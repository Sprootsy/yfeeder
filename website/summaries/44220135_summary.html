I used AI-powered calorie counting apps, and they were even worse than expected

The article from Lifehacker discusses the findings of a study published in *JAMA Network Open* which evaluated the accuracy of AI-powered calorie counting apps. Researchers at the University of Pennsylvania conducted a study comparing the performance of four popular AI-driven nutrition apps (Lose It!, MyFitnessPal, Nutritionix Track, and Samsung Health) against a registered dietitian's manual calorie counts. The apps were assessed based on their ability to estimate calories and macronutrients (protein, carbohydrates, and fat) in a variety of meals, and the error rates were calculated.

The study revealed that these AI-powered apps, which rely on users uploading photos of their meals, frequently underestimated calorie counts, particularly for more complex dishes. The apps generally performed better with single-item foods but struggled significantly with mixed meals and those lacking clear visual references for portion size. The study highlighted a tendency for the apps to underestimate calorie content more often than overestimating it. This is a problematic issue for users who depend on these apps for weight management or specific dietary needs, as it could lead to underconsumption and hinder their health goals.

Specifically, the error rates varied widely depending on the app and the meal type. The researchers found that macronutrient estimations were even less accurate than calorie counts. This is concerning as macronutrient tracking is vital for individuals following specific diets such as ketogenic or high-protein diets. The inconsistency in accuracy undermines the reliability of using these apps as precise tools for dietary tracking.

The article also points out that while AI technology has advanced considerably, challenges remain in accurately assessing food volume, portion sizes, and ingredient composition from images alone. The variability in lighting, camera angles, and food presentation impacts the ability of these apps to provide consistent and dependable results. The inherent limitations of image-based estimations are further compounded by the algorithms' dependency on a comprehensive and accurate food database, which may not always be up-to-date or complete.

The Lifehacker piece suggests that users should be cautious when relying solely on AI-powered calorie counting apps for their nutritional information. It recommends that individuals consider these apps as supplementary tools rather than definitive sources. The article promotes the potential benefits of consulting with registered dietitians or nutritionists for personalized dietary advice and accurate meal planning. It underscores the importance of using multiple methods for tracking food intake and being aware of the limitations of technology in nutrition management.

In conclusion, while AI-powered calorie counting apps offer convenience, the study indicates that they are not yet reliable enough to replace traditional methods or professional guidance. Users should be aware of their potential inaccuracies and use them with caution, supplementing their use with professional advice and other methods of dietary tracking.

Valve Announces New Steam Machine, Steam Controller and Steam Frame

Summary:

According to a recent report, Valve's Steam Machines, which were an attempt to bring PC gaming to the living room, are finally being delisted from Steam in 2024. The Steam Machines initiative was introduced in 2013 with the goal of providing pre-built, Linux-based gaming PCs that could seamlessly integrate with the Steam platform and offer a console-like experience. However, the project failed to gain traction and was largely considered a failure. The Steam Machines were intended to offer an open and customizable alternative to traditional gaming consoles. They ran on SteamOS, a Linux distribution developed by Valve, and were designed to be plug-and-play devices that could be easily connected to a TV and used with a Steam Controller. Multiple manufacturers, including Alienware, Zotac, and Asus, produced Steam Machines with varying specifications and price points. Despite the initial excitement and promise, Steam Machines faced numerous challenges. One of the main issues was the limited availability of games that were natively compatible with SteamOS. While Steam offered a large library of games, many of them were designed for Windows and required compatibility layers like Proton to run on Linux. This resulted in performance issues and a less-than-optimal gaming experience for some titles. Another challenge was the lack of awareness and marketing for Steam Machines. Unlike established console brands like PlayStation and Xbox, Steam Machines struggled to gain mainstream recognition and consumer interest. Furthermore, the Steam Controller, which was designed to be the primary input device for Steam Machines, received mixed reviews. While it offered unique features like haptic feedback and customizable controls, it had a steep learning curve and was not always the ideal choice for all types of games. The controller's unconventional design and lack of familiarity made it difficult for some gamers to adapt to it. Over time, Valve gradually shifted its focus away from Steam Machines and towards other projects. The company continued to develop and improve SteamOS, but it also invested in technologies like Proton and Steam Play, which allowed Windows games to run on Linux with minimal performance impact. This approach made it easier for gamers to play their existing libraries on Linux without having to rely on native SteamOS games. Now, the delisting of Steam Machines from Steam marks the final nail in the coffin for this ambitious project. While Steam Machines may not have achieved their original goals, they played a significant role in advancing Linux gaming and paving the way for future innovations. The lessons learned from Steam Machines have also informed Valve's subsequent projects, such as the Steam Deck, which has been far more successful in bringing PC gaming to a portable form factor.

Key points:

  • Steam Machines are being delisted from Steam in 2024.
  • Steam Machines were Valve's attempt to bring PC gaming to the living room.
  • Steam Machines ran on SteamOS, a Linux distribution developed by Valve.
  • The project failed to gain traction due to limited game compatibility, lack of awareness, and issues with the Steam Controller.
  • Valve shifted its focus to technologies like Proton and Steam Play.
  • The delisting marks the end of the Steam Machines project.
  • Steam Machines played a role in advancing Linux gaming.

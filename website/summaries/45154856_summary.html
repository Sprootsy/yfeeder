Navy SEALs reportedly killed North Korean fishermen to hide a failed mission

According to the New York Times article from September 5, 2025, details have emerged about a covert mission involving a Navy SEAL team sent to North Korea in 2019. The mission, authorized by then-President Donald Trump, aimed to gather intelligence and potentially disrupt North Korea's nuclear weapons program.

The article details that the SEAL team infiltrated North Korea via submarine and proceeded to a known nuclear facility. Their primary objective was reconnaissance, to confirm the facility's operational status and collect samples of nuclear material. However, the mission's parameters reportedly shifted mid-operation, allegedly on direct orders from President Trump, to include sabotage of the facility if the opportunity presented itself.

The mission faced several critical challenges. The SEAL team encountered unexpected security measures at the nuclear site, including advanced surveillance technology and a larger-than-anticipated contingent of North Korean guards. This complicated their initial reconnaissance efforts. The weather also played a significant role, with a sudden typhoon disrupting communication lines and hindering their planned extraction.

The article indicates that the mission ultimately failed to achieve its secondary objective of sabotage due to the heightened security and the operational difficulties caused by the typhoon. While the team managed to collect some intelligence and samples, they were forced to abort the mission prematurely to avoid detection and capture.

The extraction process was fraught with peril. The SEAL team's designated pickup point was compromised, forcing them to improvise and navigate through hostile territory to reach an alternate extraction site. During this escape, a firefight ensued with North Korean forces, resulting in casualties on both sides.

The article further reports on the political fallout from the failed mission. The operation was kept secret from key members of the Trump administration, including the Secretary of Defense and the Director of National Intelligence, raising concerns about the decision-making process and the potential for escalation with North Korea.

The New York Times investigation reveals that the mission's failure exacerbated tensions between the United States and North Korea, leading to a period of increased military posturing and stalled diplomatic efforts. The long-term consequences of the operation are still being felt, according to the article, as it has complicated efforts to denuclearize the Korean Peninsula and has raised questions about the use of covert military operations in foreign policy.

The article also highlights the ethical considerations surrounding the mission, including the potential violation of international law and the risks to the SEAL team involved.

Key Points:

  • In 2019, President Trump authorized a covert Navy SEAL mission to North Korea targeting a nuclear facility.
  • The mission's objectives included reconnaissance and potential sabotage.
  • The SEAL team faced unexpected security measures, a typhoon, and communication disruptions.
  • The sabotage objective was not achieved, and the team was forced to abort the mission.
  • The extraction was difficult, involving a firefight with North Korean forces.
  • Key members of the Trump administration were kept in the dark about the mission.
  • The mission strained US-North Korea relations and complicated denuclearization efforts.
  • The operation raised ethical concerns and questions about international law.

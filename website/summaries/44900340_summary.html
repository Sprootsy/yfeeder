Is chain-of-thought AI reasoning a mirage?

Summary:

The article "Real Reasoning" by Sean Goedecke explores the complexities of human reasoning and decision-making, contrasting it with the idealized, purely logical models often assumed in fields like economics and artificial intelligence. Goedecke argues that real-world reasoning is far more nuanced and incorporates a variety of factors beyond strict logic, including emotions, context, and social considerations.

The author begins by highlighting the limitations of traditional rational choice theory, which posits that individuals make decisions by weighing costs and benefits to maximize their utility. He points out that this model often fails to predict or explain actual human behavior. People frequently make choices that appear irrational from a purely logical standpoint, such as sticking with a losing investment or acting altruistically even when it's not in their self-interest.

Goedecke suggests that emotions play a crucial role in reasoning. Feelings like fear, anger, and love can significantly influence our judgments and decisions, often leading us to deviate from purely rational calculations. He also emphasizes the importance of context. The way a problem is framed, the social environment, and our past experiences all shape how we approach and solve problems. He gives the example of playing poker, where purely logical deductions are not enough to be succesful, and understanding human behaviour and thinking is important.

Furthermore, the article delves into the social aspects of reasoning. Humans are social creatures, and our decisions are often influenced by the desire to maintain relationships, conform to social norms, and avoid social disapproval. We also rely on trust and cooperation, which are not always based on strict logical assessments. We tend to conform to avoid being excluded from groups.

Goedecke contrasts human reasoning with the kind of reasoning that is currently possible with AI. He argues that while AI can excel at tasks requiring logical computation and pattern recognition, it often struggles with tasks that require common sense, emotional intelligence, and social awareness. He also mentions the importance of reflection and introspection in humans, to better understand ourselves.

The author concludes that understanding the full spectrum of human reasoning—including its emotional, contextual, and social dimensions—is essential for building more realistic models of decision-making and for developing AI systems that can truly understand and interact with the world in a human-like way. Real reasoning is messy, complex, and deeply intertwined with our emotions and social lives.

Key Points:

  • Rational choice theory is an incomplete model of human reasoning.
  • Emotions significantly influence our judgments and decisions.
  • Context and framing play a crucial role in how we approach problems.
  • Social considerations, such as maintaining relationships and conforming to norms, affect our choices.
  • Real reasoning includes emotions, context and social considerations.
  • Current AI systems lack the emotional intelligence and social awareness necessary for true human-like reasoning.

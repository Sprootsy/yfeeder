Best practices for dealing with human waste in the great outdoors

The article discusses the proper methods for defecating outdoors in order to minimize environmental impact and prevent the spread of disease. It emphasizes the importance of selecting a suitable location away from water sources, trails, and campsites. The article recommends digging a cathole that is 6-8 inches deep and at least 200 feet (approximately 70 big steps) away from water sources, trails, and campsites.

It details the use of a trowel or small shovel for digging, and the necessity of burying human waste and toilet paper. The article also mentions packing out toilet paper or using natural materials if burying is not possible. It cautions against burying disposable wipes as they do not decompose easily. Proper hand hygiene after defecation is stressed, using either hand sanitizer or thorough hand washing with soap and water, if available.

The article highlights that in certain environments, such as alpine or desert areas, or when in a group, packing out solid human waste may be required. It also touches upon the importance of being aware of specific regulations and guidelines for waste disposal in the area you are visiting. The key goal is to leave no trace and protect the environment for future visitors.

Key Points:

  • Location: Choose a spot at least 200 feet away from water sources, trails, and campsites.
  • Cathole: Dig a cathole 6-8 inches deep using a trowel or small shovel.
  • Bury Waste: Bury human waste and toilet paper in the cathole.
  • Pack it out: In some settings you will need to pack out your poop.
  • Toilet Paper: Pack out toilet paper or use natural materials if burying is not feasible.
  • Avoid Wipes: Do not bury disposable wipes; pack them out.
  • Hand Hygiene: Clean hands thoroughly with hand sanitizer or soap and water after going to the bathroom.
  • Regulations: Be aware of specific waste disposal regulations for the area.
  • Leave No Trace: Minimize environmental impact and protect the environment.

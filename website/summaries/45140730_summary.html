European Commission fines Google €2.95B over abusive ad tech practices

The European Commission has decided to initiate infringement proceedings against six Member States—Belgium, Germany, Spain, France, Italy, and Luxembourg—regarding their implementation of Council Directive 85/374/EEC concerning liability for defective products. This Directive establishes the principle of liability without fault for producers, meaning that a producer is liable for damage caused by a defect in their product, regardless of whether negligence was involved. The Commission has identified several areas where the national laws of these Member States appear to be inconsistent with the Directive, potentially undermining consumer protection and creating unequal conditions of competition within the internal market.

Specifically, the concerns relate to the definition of a 'producer,' the scope of liability concerning primary agricultural products, limitations on the total liability for damage caused by identical products with the same defect, and restrictions on the period during which a producer can be held liable. For example, some national laws may not adequately define who is considered a 'producer,' which could leave consumers uncertain about whom to hold responsible for defective imported goods. Additionally, some Member States might have introduced exemptions or limitations for primary agricultural products that are not in line with the Directive's intent. The Directive aims to provide a harmonized level of protection for consumers across the EU, and discrepancies in national implementation could lead to varying levels of protection and distortions in the market.

The Commission's decision to launch these proceedings reflects its commitment to ensuring the correct and consistent application of EU law throughout the Union. The initial stage of the infringement procedure involves sending a letter of formal notice to the Member States concerned, requesting them to submit their observations on the alleged infringements. The Member States have two months to respond. If the Commission is not satisfied with the responses or if the Member States fail to take corrective action, it may issue a reasoned opinion, which is a formal request to comply with EU law. If the Member States still fail to comply, the Commission can ultimately refer the case to the Court of Justice of the European Union, which has the power to issue binding judgments and impose financial penalties.

This action underscores the importance of the product liability directive in safeguarding consumer rights and ensuring fair competition. By addressing inconsistencies in national laws, the Commission aims to create a level playing field for businesses and enhance consumer confidence in the safety of products available in the EU market.

Key points:

  • The European Commission is taking legal action against Belgium, Germany, Spain, France, Italy, and Luxembourg.
  • The action concerns the incorrect implementation of the EU's product liability directive (85/374/EEC).
  • The directive establishes liability without fault for producers of defective products.
  • The Commission identified inconsistencies in national laws regarding the definition of 'producer,' the scope of liability for agricultural products, limitations on liability, and time limits for claims.
  • The Commission is concerned that these inconsistencies undermine consumer protection and create unfair competition.
  • The proceedings begin with a letter of formal notice, giving the Member States two months to respond.
  • Failure to comply could lead to the case being referred to the Court of Justice of the European Union.
  • The aim is to ensure consistent application of EU law, protect consumers, and ensure fair competition.

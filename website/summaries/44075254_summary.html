Types of optical systems in a lens designer's toolbox (2020)

The article discusses lens design forms and their impact on optical performance. It explores various lens shapes, including plano-convex, bi-convex, plano-concave, bi-concave, meniscus, and aspheric lenses, detailing their characteristics, advantages, and disadvantages in different applications. The article also covers lens combinations like doublets and triplets, emphasizing their role in aberration correction and image quality improvement. Manufacturing considerations and the use of different glass types to achieve specific optical properties are addressed. The article concludes by highlighting the importance of selecting the appropriate lens design form based on application requirements, performance goals, and cost constraints.

Key Points:

  • Lens Forms: Different lens shapes (plano-convex, bi-convex, plano-concave, bi-concave, meniscus) offer distinct optical properties.
  • Plano-Convex Lenses: Suitable for focusing parallel light rays and minimizing spherical aberration when the curved side faces the object.
  • Bi-Convex Lenses: Offer shorter focal lengths and are useful for collecting and focusing light.
  • Plano-Concave Lenses: Diverge light and are often used to expand beam diameters or increase focal length of existing systems.
  • Bi-Concave Lenses: Provide greater divergence and are useful for reducing image size or increasing the focal length of another lens.
  • Meniscus Lenses: Can be either positive or negative and are used to reduce aberrations, especially when used in combination with other lenses.
  • Aspheric Lenses: Offer superior aberration correction compared to spherical lenses, allowing for sharper images and higher performance in demanding applications.
  • Doublets and Triplets: Lens combinations used to correct chromatic and spherical aberrations, improving overall image quality.
  • Glass Types: Different glass types (e.g., crown and flint) have varying refractive indices and dispersion characteristics, influencing lens performance.
  • Manufacturing Considerations: Lens form affects manufacturing complexity and cost; simpler shapes are generally easier and cheaper to produce.
  • Application-Specific Selection: Choosing the right lens form depends on the specific application, desired performance, and budget constraints.
  • Aberration Correction: Different lens forms and combinations help minimize aberrations like spherical aberration, coma, and chromatic aberration.

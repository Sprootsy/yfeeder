I was a Theranos whistleblower. Here's what I think Elizabeth Holmes is up to

Tyler Shultz, the grandson of former Secretary of State George Shultz, reflects on his experiences as a whistleblower at Theranos and the lessons learned from the rise and fall of the company in an article published on STAT News. Shultz joined Theranos shortly after graduating from college, drawn to the company's mission of revolutionizing blood testing. Initially, he was excited by the prospect of working on innovative technology that could potentially save lives.

However, Shultz quickly became disillusioned as he witnessed firsthand the inaccuracies and inconsistencies in Theranos' technology. He observed that the company's blood-testing devices, which were designed to perform a wide range of tests using just a few drops of blood, were prone to errors and often produced unreliable results. He and other employees raised concerns about the accuracy of the tests, but their concerns were often dismissed or ignored by Theranos' leadership, including CEO Elizabeth Holmes and COO Sunny Balwani.

As Shultz delved deeper into the inner workings of Theranos, he uncovered a pattern of deception and mismanagement. He discovered that the company was using traditional blood-testing methods for the majority of its tests, rather than its own proprietary technology. He also learned that Theranos was manipulating test results to make them appear more accurate than they actually were.

Faced with a moral dilemma, Shultz made the difficult decision to become a whistleblower. He shared his concerns with his grandfather, George Shultz, who was a member of Theranos' board of directors. Initially, George Shultz dismissed his grandson's allegations, siding with Elizabeth Holmes, whom he had come to admire and trust.

Undeterred, Tyler Shultz contacted a reporter at The Wall Street Journal, John Carreyrou, and provided him with evidence of Theranos' fraudulent practices. Carreyrou's subsequent exposé in The Wall Street Journal in 2015 triggered a series of investigations by regulatory agencies, including the Food and Drug Administration (FDA) and the Centers for Medicare & Medicaid Services (CMS).

These investigations ultimately led to the downfall of Theranos. The company's blood-testing devices were found to be inaccurate and unreliable, and its laboratory practices were deemed to be substandard. Theranos was eventually forced to shut down its operations, and Elizabeth Holmes and Sunny Balwani were charged with multiple counts of fraud.

Shultz reflects on the personal toll that his decision to become a whistleblower took on him and his family. His relationship with his grandfather was strained, and he faced intense pressure from Theranos' lawyers, who attempted to discredit him and silence him. Despite these challenges, Shultz says he has no regrets about his decision to come forward. He believes that it was the right thing to do, both for the patients who were potentially harmed by Theranos' inaccurate blood tests and for the integrity of the scientific community.

Shultz concludes by emphasizing the importance of ethical leadership and corporate responsibility. He argues that companies should prioritize patient safety and scientific integrity over profits and personal ambition. He also calls for greater oversight of the healthcare industry to prevent similar scandals from happening in the future.

The article also briefly mentions Billy Evans, Elizabeth Holmes' partner, and his recent venture, Haemanthus, a company working on software for clinical trials, and touches on the complexities of moving on from the Theranos scandal.

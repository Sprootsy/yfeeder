IP blocking the UK is not enough to comply with the Online Safety Act

The article discusses the complexities and potential inadequacies of IP blocking as a measure to comply with the UK's Online Safety Act (OSA). It primarily focuses on a presentation given to Ofcom by a company specializing in circumventing IP blocks, highlighting the limitations of IP blocking as a standalone solution for content moderation and online safety. The author analyzes the presentation's claims and extrapolates on the broader implications for implementing the OSA effectively.

The presentation underscored that IP blocking, while seemingly straightforward, is easily bypassed using various techniques. These techniques include the use of VPNs, proxies, and other methods that effectively mask a user's true IP address, making it appear as if they are accessing the internet from a different location. The presenter argued that relying solely on IP blocking to prevent UK users from accessing harmful content would be largely ineffective, as a significant portion of the user base could circumvent these blocks with relative ease.

The author notes that this ease of circumvention poses a significant challenge for Ofcom, the UK's communications regulator, which is tasked with enforcing the OSA. If IP blocking is the primary mechanism for preventing access to illegal or harmful content, a large segment of the UK population will still be able to access it, undermining the purpose of the legislation. The author suggests that Ofcom needs to consider more sophisticated and robust methods of content moderation beyond simple IP blocking. The presenter even suggests different strategies for blocking.

One of the key challenges identified in the article is the tension between the goals of the OSA and the technical realities of internet infrastructure. The OSA aims to create a safer online environment for UK users, but the internet is inherently a global network, designed to facilitate the free flow of information. Attempts to restrict access to content based on geographic location are often difficult to enforce effectively, due to the ease with which users can mask their location.

The author also points out that the focus on IP blocking may be a distraction from other, potentially more effective, methods of content moderation. These methods could include content filtering based on keywords, image recognition, and behavioral analysis. Furthermore, the author highlights the potential for collaboration between online platforms and content moderation providers to develop more sophisticated tools for identifying and removing harmful content. He emphasizes the need for a multi-layered approach, combining various technical and policy measures to achieve the goals of the OSA.

Another aspect of the discussion is the potential for unintended consequences of relying too heavily on IP blocking. For example, legitimate users may be inadvertently blocked from accessing content if they are using a VPN for privacy reasons or if they are traveling abroad. This could lead to frustration and a perception that the government is overreaching in its attempts to regulate the internet.

In conclusion, the article argues that IP blocking is a flawed and insufficient solution for complying with the Online Safety Act. It highlights the ease with which IP blocks can be circumvented, the potential for unintended consequences, and the need for Ofcom to adopt a more comprehensive and nuanced approach to content moderation. The author urges Ofcom to consider a wider range of technical and policy measures, including content filtering, behavioral analysis, and collaboration with online platforms, to effectively address the challenges of online safety.

Key Points:

  • IP blocking is easily bypassed using VPNs, proxies, and other techniques.
  • Relying solely on IP blocking to comply with the Online Safety Act (OSA) is insufficient.

  • There's a tension between the goals of the OSA and the global nature of the internet.
  • Alternative content moderation methods include content filtering, image recognition, and behavioral analysis.

Migrating away from Rust

Summary:

The article discusses the challenges and considerations involved in migrating a game project away from the Rust programming language. It acknowledges Rust's strengths, such as its safety features, performance, and strong community support, but highlights situations where its complexities and specific paradigms might hinder development, especially in the context of game development. The author shares experiences and insights gained from a game project that initially embraced Rust but later decided to transition to C#. The migration was motivated by several factors, including the steep learning curve associated with Rust's borrow checker and ownership model, which led to increased development time and cognitive overhead for the team.

One of the primary issues was the difficulty in rapidly prototyping and iterating on gameplay mechanics due to Rust's strictness. The need to carefully manage memory and lifetimes often interfered with the creative process. While Rust's safety features are beneficial in preventing bugs, they can also make it harder to quickly experiment with different ideas. The author also notes the limited availability of experienced Rust game developers compared to other languages like C# and C++. This made it challenging to scale the team and find developers who could effectively contribute to the project.

Furthermore, the article addresses the ecosystem and tooling available for game development in Rust. Although the Rust game development ecosystem is growing, it still lags behind more established languages like C# in terms of mature game engines, libraries, and tools. The author points out that while there are promising Rust-based game engines, they may not offer the same level of features and support as engines like Unity or Unreal Engine.

The decision to migrate to C# was driven by the desire to improve development velocity, leverage the extensive tooling and resources available in the C# ecosystem (particularly Unity), and reduce the cognitive load on the development team. C# offers a more forgiving memory management model with garbage collection, which simplifies development and allows for faster prototyping. The author emphasizes that the migration was not an indictment of Rust itself but rather a pragmatic choice based on the specific needs and constraints of the project.

The article also touches upon the performance implications of choosing C# over Rust. While Rust is generally considered to be a faster language due to its lack of garbage collection and fine-grained control over memory, C# can still provide excellent performance, especially when combined with optimization techniques and the capabilities of modern game engines. The author suggests that the performance difference between the two languages may not be significant enough to justify the increased development complexity associated with Rust in certain cases.

In conclusion, the article presents a balanced perspective on the challenges and benefits of using Rust for game development. It highlights the importance of carefully evaluating the specific requirements of a project, the skills and experience of the development team, and the available tooling and ecosystem before committing to a particular language. While Rust can be a powerful choice for certain types of games, it may not always be the most practical option, especially when rapid prototyping, ease of development, and access to a large pool of experienced developers are critical factors.

Key Points:

  • Rust's safety features and performance are valuable but can increase development complexity, especially for rapid prototyping in game development.
  • The steep learning curve of Rust's borrow checker and ownership model can lead to increased development time and cognitive overhead.
  • The Rust game development ecosystem is still less mature compared to C#, with fewer mature game engines, libraries, and tools.
  • Migrating to C# was motivated by the desire to improve development velocity, leverage the C# ecosystem, and reduce cognitive load.
  • C# offers a more forgiving memory management model (garbage collection), simplifying development and enabling faster prototyping.
  • The performance difference between Rust and C# may not always justify the increased development complexity of Rust, depending on the project's needs.
  • The choice of programming language should be based on a careful evaluation of project requirements, team skills, and available resources.

Circadian justice (2022)

Summary

This paper explores the relationship between social media and political accountability in non-democratic regimes, focusing on the case of China. It challenges the conventional view that social media platforms are inherently democratizing forces, arguing instead that their impact is contingent on the specific political context and the strategies employed by the authoritarian state. The paper examines how the Chinese government uses a combination of censorship, propaganda, and strategic engagement to shape the online discourse and maintain control over the narrative. It argues that while social media provides citizens with new avenues to express grievances and monitor government actions, the state's sophisticated control mechanisms limit the extent to which these platforms can effectively hold officials accountable.

The paper analyzes different forms of online activism and contention in China, including online petitions, exposure of corruption, and expressions of public opinion on policy issues. It discusses how these activities are often subject to censorship and manipulation, with the state selectively allowing certain forms of criticism while suppressing others that pose a more direct threat to its authority. The paper also examines the role of social media in shaping public perceptions of government legitimacy and performance, noting that the state actively promotes positive narratives and attempts to discredit dissenting voices.

Furthermore, the paper investigates the impact of social media on local governance and the relationship between citizens and local officials. It explores how social media can facilitate citizen participation in local affairs and provide a platform for holding local officials accountable for their actions. However, it also highlights the challenges and limitations of this process, including the potential for abuse of power, the spread of misinformation, and the difficulty of translating online activism into real-world change.

In conclusion, the paper argues that social media's impact on political accountability in China is complex and multifaceted. While these platforms have created new opportunities for citizen engagement and oversight, the state's sophisticated control mechanisms effectively limit their potential to democratize the regime. The paper suggests that future research should focus on the specific strategies employed by authoritarian states to manage social media and the ways in which citizens navigate these constraints to pursue their political goals.

Key Points

  • Social media's impact on political accountability in non-democratic regimes is contingent on the political context and state strategies.
  • The Chinese government uses censorship, propaganda, and strategic engagement to control the online discourse.
  • Online activism and contention are subject to censorship and manipulation.
  • Social media shapes public perceptions of government legitimacy and performance.
  • Social media can facilitate citizen participation in local affairs, but faces challenges like abuse of power and misinformation.
  • The state's control mechanisms limit social media's potential to democratize the regime.

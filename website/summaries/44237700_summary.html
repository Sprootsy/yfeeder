Dubious Math in Infinite Jest (2009)

Summary

The article "Dubious Math in Infinite Jest" by Dave Monroe on The Howling Fantods examines mathematical inconsistencies and impossibilities within David Foster Wallace's novel Infinite Jest. The author meticulously analyzes specific instances in the book where numbers, measurements, or calculations appear to defy logic or real-world constraints.

Monroe begins by acknowledging Wallace's penchant for detail and the seemingly plausible scientific or mathematical elements incorporated into the narrative. However, he argues that a closer inspection reveals a deliberate disregard for accuracy in certain quantitative aspects.

One key area of focus is the Eschaton game. Monroe questions the feasibility of the game's playing field, particularly the hyperbolic paraboloid (hypar) surface. He investigates the mathematical implications of the described dimensions and angles, suggesting that they lead to physical impossibilities or contradictions. For instance, the calculations regarding the hypar's curvature and the players' ability to navigate it at high speeds raise concerns about the game's playability as described.

The article also delves into the timeline presented in the novel, particularly the dating system based on corporate-sponsored years. Monroe examines the sequence of these years and the ages of certain characters, identifying potential inconsistencies that challenge the reader's ability to construct a coherent chronology. He suggests that Wallace may have intentionally introduced these temporal anomalies to create a sense of disorientation or to underscore the novel's themes of societal fragmentation and the blurring of reality.

Furthermore, Monroe analyzes other numerical details scattered throughout the book, such as measurements of objects or distances, and finds similar discrepancies. He proposes that these mathematical "errors" are not simply oversights but rather deliberate artistic choices on Wallace's part. The author posits that Wallace uses these inconsistencies to disrupt the reader's expectations of realism and to emphasize the artificiality of the world he has created.

Monroe concludes by suggesting that the dubious math in Infinite Jest serves a thematic purpose. By intentionally incorporating these inconsistencies, Wallace challenges the reader to question the nature of reality and the limits of human understanding. The article emphasizes that while the novel contains elements of scientific and mathematical detail, its ultimate goal is not to provide a perfectly accurate representation of the world but rather to explore the complexities of human experience and the subjective nature of truth.

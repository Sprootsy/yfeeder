Why the Internet Is Turning to Shit

The article "Why the Internet Is Turning to Shit" by Nathan J. Robinson, published in Current Affairs, explores the perceived decline in the quality and usefulness of the internet. Robinson argues that the internet, once envisioned as a democratizing force for knowledge and connection, is increasingly becoming a frustrating, commercialized, and manipulative environment.

Robinson posits that several factors contribute to this degradation. The dominance of a few large tech companies, such as Google, Facebook (Meta), and Amazon, has led to centralization and a lack of genuine competition. These companies prioritize profit maximization over user experience, resulting in algorithm-driven content that often favors sensationalism, outrage, and misinformation. The pursuit of engagement leads to the amplification of extreme viewpoints and the creation of echo chambers, further polarizing society.

The article criticizes the advertising-based business model that fuels much of the internet. The need to attract and retain users for advertising purposes incentivizes platforms to manipulate user behavior and collect vast amounts of personal data. This data is then used to target users with increasingly personalized ads, often exploiting their vulnerabilities and contributing to a sense of constant surveillance.

Robinson also points to the decline in the quality of online content. The rise of clickbait, sponsored content, and search engine optimization (SEO) has made it increasingly difficult to find reliable and trustworthy information. The internet is now flooded with low-quality articles, duplicate content, and deceptive websites designed to generate revenue or promote specific agendas.

The author laments the loss of the internet's early promise of decentralization and community. The consolidation of power in the hands of a few tech giants has stifled innovation and reduced user autonomy. The rise of social media has also contributed to a culture of online bullying, harassment, and political polarization.

While acknowledging the continued existence of valuable resources and communities online, Robinson expresses concern about the overall trajectory of the internet. He suggests that a fundamental shift in values and priorities is needed to reclaim the internet's potential as a force for good. This shift would involve prioritizing user well-being over profit, promoting decentralization and competition, and fostering a culture of critical thinking and media literacy.

Key Points:

  • The internet is perceived to be declining in quality and usefulness due to commercialization, centralization, and manipulation.
  • Dominance of large tech companies (Google, Facebook, Amazon) prioritizes profit over user experience.
  • Algorithm-driven content favors sensationalism, outrage, and misinformation.
  • Advertising-based business model incentivizes user manipulation and data collection.
  • Decline in content quality due to clickbait, sponsored content, and SEO.
  • Loss of decentralization and community due to consolidation of power.
  • Social media contributes to online bullying, harassment, and political polarization.
  • A shift in values is needed to reclaim the internet's potential for good.

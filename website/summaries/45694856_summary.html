First shape found that can't pass through itself

Summary:

The article discusses the discovery of the first "anhiral" shape, a three-dimensional object that cannot pass through its mirror image without being broken or fundamentally altered. This concept is rooted in the mathematical field of topology, which studies properties that remain unchanged under continuous deformations like stretching or bending. For decades, mathematicians have sought such a shape, one that is topologically distinct from its reflection. The search involved analyzing knots and their mirror images, as knots can be considered embeddings of a circle in 3D space. However, a true anhiral object needs to be a solid, occupying a volume. The breakthrough came from a team of mathematicians who designed a complex, twisted shape using computer simulations and mathematical proofs to demonstrate its anhirality. This shape, resembling a distorted pretzel, contains intricate loops and twists carefully designed to prevent it from smoothly transforming into its mirror image. The article explains the mathematical concepts underlying this discovery, including chirality (handedness) and the challenges of proving that a shape is truly anhiral. The finding has implications for various scientific fields, including chemistry and materials science, where the handedness of molecules and structures plays a crucial role. The anhiral object offers a novel example of how topology can manifest in real-world objects, potentially influencing the design of new materials and technologies.

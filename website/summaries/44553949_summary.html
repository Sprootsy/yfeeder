Big Data was used to see if TCM was scientific (2023)

This article from McGill University's Office for Science and Society critically examines a recent systematic review and meta-analysis that purportedly vindicated Traditional Chinese Medicine (TCM) for treating certain conditions. The author, Dr. Jonathan Jarry, argues that the review's conclusion is overly optimistic and that a closer look at the methodology reveals significant flaws. The review, published in a reputable journal, analyzed studies on TCM treatments for a range of ailments, including cardiovascular diseases, diabetes, and respiratory illnesses. While the authors of the review claimed to have found evidence supporting the efficacy of TCM, Dr. Jarry contends that their analysis suffers from several critical shortcomings.

One major issue is the inclusion of studies with questionable methodological rigor. Many of the included studies were conducted in China, where there may be biases in favor of TCM. Furthermore, the studies often lacked proper controls, blinding, and randomization, making it difficult to determine whether the observed effects were truly due to the TCM treatments or other factors. The article also points out that the review included a wide variety of TCM treatments, making it challenging to draw definitive conclusions about the effectiveness of TCM as a whole. The author emphasizes that TCM is not a monolithic entity, and different TCM practices may have varying degrees of effectiveness (or lack thereof) for different conditions.

The article highlights the problem of publication bias, where studies with positive results are more likely to be published than those with negative or null results. This can lead to an overestimation of the true effectiveness of TCM treatments. Dr. Jarry also criticizes the review's reliance on subjective outcome measures, such as patient-reported symptoms, which are more susceptible to bias than objective measures like blood pressure or cholesterol levels. Another criticism is the potential for confounding factors to influence the results of the included studies. For example, patients receiving TCM treatments may also be making other lifestyle changes, such as improving their diet or exercising more, which could contribute to any observed improvements.

Dr. Jarry concludes that while the review may provide some preliminary evidence suggesting that certain TCM treatments may have potential benefits, the evidence is far from conclusive. He argues that more rigorous research, using well-designed and well-controlled studies, is needed to determine the true efficacy of TCM. He cautions against interpreting the review as a definitive vindication of TCM, emphasizing the importance of critical thinking and evidence-based decision-making when it comes to healthcare.

Key Points:

  • A systematic review and meta-analysis claiming to vindicate TCM is critically examined.
  • The review included studies with questionable methodological rigor, potentially introducing bias.
  • The inclusion of a wide variety of TCM treatments makes it difficult to draw definitive conclusions.
  • Publication bias may have led to an overestimation of TCM's effectiveness.
  • The review relied on subjective outcome measures, which are more susceptible to bias.
  • Confounding factors may have influenced the results of the included studies.
  • More rigorous research is needed to determine the true efficacy of TCM.
  • The review should not be interpreted as a definitive vindication of TCM.

IDEs we had 30 years ago and lost (2023)

The article, "The IDEs We Had 30 Years Ago and the IDEs We Have Today," reflects on the evolution of Integrated Development Environments (IDEs) over the past three decades, contrasting the IDEs of the early 1990s with modern IDEs. The author shares experiences using various IDEs from that era, such as Turbo Pascal, Turbo C++, and Smalltalk environments, highlighting their capabilities and limitations.

The author reminisces about the speed and efficiency of early IDEs, noting that they were often optimized to run on less powerful hardware. Turbo Pascal, for example, was known for its rapid compilation times. These IDEs typically provided features like syntax highlighting, basic debugging tools, and integrated compilers, all within a single, relatively lightweight application. The author emphasizes the deep integration between the editor, compiler, and debugger, which facilitated a smooth development workflow.

However, the article also acknowledges the limitations of these early IDEs. They often lacked advanced features such as sophisticated code completion, refactoring tools, and extensive support for large projects. Debugging capabilities were more basic, and integration with version control systems was either non-existent or rudimentary. The user interfaces were generally simpler, reflecting the constraints of the hardware and software environments of the time.

The piece then contrasts these early IDEs with modern IDEs like Visual Studio, IntelliJ IDEA, and Eclipse. Modern IDEs are characterized by their extensive feature sets, including advanced code analysis, intelligent code completion, powerful debugging tools, and seamless integration with a wide range of tools and technologies. They support multiple programming languages and frameworks, and provide robust support for large-scale software development.

Modern IDEs offer features such as refactoring, static analysis, and advanced debugging capabilities that were unimaginable in the early 1990s. The integration with version control systems like Git is now a standard feature, enabling collaborative development and efficient management of code changes. The user interfaces have also evolved to become more sophisticated, offering customizable layouts and a wealth of visual cues to aid developers.

Despite the advancements, the author notes some trade-offs. Modern IDEs can be resource-intensive, requiring significant processing power and memory. Their complexity can also be overwhelming for new users. The article raises the question of whether the added features always translate into increased productivity, or if the simplicity and speed of early IDEs offered a more streamlined development experience in some contexts.

The author reflects on the changing landscape of software development, noting that modern IDEs are designed to support the complexities of contemporary software projects. These projects often involve large teams, distributed architectures, and a multitude of dependencies. Modern IDEs provide the tools and infrastructure needed to manage this complexity.

In conclusion, the article provides a nostalgic look at the IDEs of the past, while also acknowledging the significant advancements in modern IDE technology. It encourages reflection on the trade-offs between simplicity and feature richness, and prompts consideration of how IDEs can continue to evolve to meet the changing needs of software developers.

Key Points:

  • Early IDEs (e.g., Turbo Pascal, Turbo C++) were fast, efficient, and deeply integrated but lacked advanced features.
  • Modern IDEs (e.g., Visual Studio, IntelliJ IDEA, Eclipse) offer extensive feature sets, including advanced code completion, refactoring tools, and powerful debugging capabilities.
  • Early IDEs were optimized for less powerful hardware, whereas modern IDEs are resource-intensive.
  • Modern IDEs provide seamless integration with version control systems and support for large-scale software development.
  • The article questions whether the added features of modern IDEs always translate into increased productivity compared to the simplicity of earlier IDEs.
  • The evolution of IDEs reflects the increasing complexity of software development projects over the past three decades.

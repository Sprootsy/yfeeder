Medieval Africans had a unique process for purifying gold with glass (2019)

Summary

The Atlas Obscura article, "Medieval African Gold," explores the history and significance of gold production and trade in medieval West Africa, particularly focusing on the Ghana Empire and later the Mali Empire. It details how these empires controlled vast goldfields and leveraged this resource to become major economic powers, influencing trade routes and interactions across North Africa, the Middle East, and Europe. The article describes how the Ghana Empire, despite not being located in the present-day country of Ghana, flourished between the 8th and 11th centuries. It derived its power from controlling gold mines and taxing the gold trade. Arab scholars and traders documented Ghana's wealth, which was a source of fascination. The article notes the sophisticated system Ghana had developed for managing gold production and trade, including a silent barter system in some areas to protect the source of the gold. The Mali Empire, which rose to prominence in the 13th century, built upon Ghana's legacy and expanded its control over even larger gold-producing regions. Mansa Musa, the ruler of the Mali Empire, is highlighted for his famous pilgrimage to Mecca in 1324, during which he distributed so much gold that he caused inflation in Cairo and other cities along his route. This event showcased Mali's immense wealth to the world. The article also details the presence of gold in architecture, like the roof of Mansa Musa's palace. The article also explains the impact of West African gold on European economies, particularly during the late medieval period when Europe faced a gold shortage. West African gold helped to fuel the Renaissance and European trade, with Portuguese explorers eventually seeking to bypass the established trans-Saharan trade routes to access the gold directly. The article also notes the darker side of this history, as the European pursuit of gold eventually led to the exploitation and colonization of Africa. The article also emphasizes the importance of understanding the complex systems of trade, governance, and cultural exchange that existed in medieval West Africa and how gold played a central role in shaping these societies and their interactions with the wider world. It highlights the need to move beyond simplistic narratives of Africa as merely a source of raw materials and recognize the sophisticated economic and political structures that existed long before European contact.

Key Points

  • Medieval West African empires, particularly Ghana and Mali, controlled vast goldfields and became major economic powers.
  • The Ghana Empire (8th-11th centuries) derived its power from controlling gold mines and taxing the gold trade, documented by Arab scholars.
  • The Mali Empire (13th century), notably under Mansa Musa, expanded control over gold-producing regions.
  • Mansa Musa's pilgrimage to Mecca in 1324 showcased Mali's immense wealth and caused inflation due to his gold distribution.
  • West African gold impacted European economies, fueling the Renaissance and European trade during a gold shortage.
  • European pursuit of gold led to the exploitation and colonization of Africa, seeking to bypass trans-Saharan trade routes.
  • Medieval West Africa had sophisticated systems of trade, governance, and cultural exchange centered around gold.

Medical cannabis patient data exposed by unsecured database

Summary

An unsecured database belonging to a cannabis software company, THSuite, exposed the private medical information of over 30,000 medical cannabis patients across the United States. The database, which was left without a password or any security protections, contained highly sensitive data, including patients' full names, dates of birth, home addresses, phone numbers, email addresses, cannabis purchase histories, the amounts they purchased, and the medical conditions for which they were prescribed cannabis. In some instances, even scanned copies of patients' driver's licenses and medical cannabis recommendations were exposed.

The breach was discovered by security researchers Noam Rotem and Ran Locher, who promptly notified THSuite about the vulnerability. THSuite, a company providing seed-to-sale tracking software for the cannabis industry, is used by dispensaries to manage inventory and patient information, raising serious questions about data protection practices within the burgeoning cannabis sector. The exposed data originated from several states where medical cannabis is legal, including Arkansas, Oklahoma, Louisiana and Pennsylvania.

The lack of security on the database meant that anyone with basic technical skills could have accessed the patient information, potentially leading to identity theft, blackmail, or other malicious activities. Given the stigma still associated with cannabis use in some circles, the disclosure of patients' medical conditions and cannabis use could have significant personal and professional repercussions.

Following notification from the researchers, THSuite secured the database. However, the incident highlights the critical need for stricter data security measures within the cannabis industry, particularly as it handles sensitive patient information subject to HIPAA-like regulations in many states. It also underscores the broader importance of robust data protection practices for companies handling personal information, especially in sectors dealing with health-related data.

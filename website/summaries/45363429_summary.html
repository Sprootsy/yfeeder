Tinder, Hinge, and their corporate owner keep rape under wraps

Summary:

The Markup investigation reveals that Match Group, the parent company of popular dating apps like Tinder and Hinge, allegedly suppressed internal research that indicated their platforms could be harmful and were potentially designed in ways that promoted addictive behaviors. The investigation is based on a cache of confidential documents, including internal memos, presentations, and emails, spanning several years. These documents suggest that Match Group was aware of the potential for harm, including increased feelings of loneliness, anxiety, and depression among users, as well as the risk of sexual assault.

According to the article, Match Group initiated multiple internal studies to examine the impact of its apps on users' mental health and well-being. One study, called the "Happiness Project," sought to quantify how dating app usage affected users' overall happiness. The findings allegedly revealed a correlation between heavy app use and negative mental health outcomes. Despite these findings, the investigation claims that Match Group leadership chose to downplay or suppress the research, fearing it would damage the company's image and stock price.

The Markup also suggests that Match Group actively worked to avoid regulatory scrutiny and deflect concerns raised by advocacy groups and researchers. The company allegedly lobbied against legislation aimed at protecting dating app users and resisted calls for greater transparency about its algorithms and data practices. The article further alleges that Match Group prioritized user engagement and monetization over user safety and well-being, employing tactics designed to keep users hooked on the apps for as long as possible. These tactics allegedly included features that promote compulsive swiping and notifications designed to lure users back to the app.

Moreover, the investigation raises concerns about the safety of users, particularly women, on Match Group's platforms. Internal documents reportedly show that the company was aware of the prevalence of sexual harassment and assault on its apps but did not take adequate steps to address the problem. The Markup alleges that Match Group prioritized protecting its brand over ensuring the safety of its users, failing to implement robust reporting mechanisms or ban known offenders from its platforms.

In response to The Markup's investigation, Match Group has denied the allegations, claiming that it takes user safety and well-being seriously. The company has also defended its research practices, arguing that its internal studies are conducted ethically and transparently. However, The Markup stands by its reporting, citing the extensive documentation it obtained as evidence of Match Group's alleged wrongdoing.

Key Points:

  • Match Group allegedly suppressed internal research showing its dating apps (Tinder, Hinge) could be harmful to users' mental health, leading to feelings of loneliness, anxiety, and depression.
  • The company prioritized user engagement and monetization over user safety and well-being, allegedly employing tactics designed to keep users hooked on the apps.
  • Match Group allegedly lobbied against legislation aimed at protecting dating app users and resisted calls for greater transparency about its algorithms and data practices.
  • Internal documents reportedly show that Match Group was aware of the prevalence of sexual harassment and assault on its apps but did not take adequate steps to address the problem.
  • Match Group denies the allegations, claiming it takes user safety and well-being seriously and conducts ethical research.

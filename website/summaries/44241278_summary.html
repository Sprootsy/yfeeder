Show HN: I made a 3D printed VTOL drone

The article describes the author's journey of designing and building a 3D-printed VTOL (Vertical Take-Off and Landing) aircraft. The project started with the desire to create a simple and robust VTOL capable of both hovering and forward flight. The author initially considered tail-sitter designs but ultimately opted for a quadplane configuration, which uses four vertical propellers for hovering and a separate pusher propeller for forward flight. This design choice allowed for simpler control and a more direct transition between hover and forward flight modes.

The design process heavily utilized Fusion 360 for CAD modeling and involved numerous iterations to optimize the aircraft's aerodynamics, structural integrity, and weight distribution. The author experimented with different wing designs, airfoil profiles, and motor placements. Computational Fluid Dynamics (CFD) simulations were used to analyze airflow and optimize the wing shape for efficient forward flight. The aircraft's frame was designed to be 3D-printed in lightweight yet durable materials like LW-PLA, focusing on modularity and ease of assembly. Internal components, such as the flight controller, battery, and ESCs (Electronic Speed Controllers), were carefully chosen and positioned to achieve a balanced center of gravity.

The author also details the challenges encountered during the build, including issues with motor synchronization, vibration, and control tuning. The initial flights revealed problems with the transition between hover and forward flight, requiring adjustments to the flight controller settings and motor configurations. The author implemented custom mixing algorithms in the flight controller to improve stability and control authority during the transition phase. Furthermore, the article goes into detail about the electrical and control system setup, including the wiring, power distribution, and flight controller configuration. The author used an ArduPilot flight controller, which provided a flexible platform for customizing the aircraft's behavior.

The article highlights the importance of iterative testing and refinement in the development process. Each flight provided valuable data and insights that were used to improve the aircraft's design and performance. The author also emphasizes the role of community support and open-source resources in enabling the project's success. The final version of the VTOL aircraft successfully demonstrated stable hovering, smooth transitions to forward flight, and controlled maneuvering. The project showcased the potential of 3D printing and open-source software in creating custom aerial vehicles. The author expressed satisfaction with the results and intends to continue exploring new designs and functionalities in future projects.

Key Points:

  • The author designed and built a 3D-printed VTOL aircraft with a quadplane configuration.
  • The design process involved extensive CAD modeling in Fusion 360 and CFD simulations to optimize aerodynamics.
  • The aircraft was 3D-printed using lightweight and durable materials like LW-PLA.
  • Challenges included motor synchronization, vibration, and control tuning, particularly during the transition between hover and forward flight.
  • An ArduPilot flight controller was used with custom mixing algorithms to improve stability.
  • Iterative testing and refinement were crucial for achieving successful flights.
  • The project demonstrates the potential of 3D printing and open-source software in creating custom VTOL aircraft.

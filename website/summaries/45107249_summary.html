A gentle introduction to CP/M

This article serves as an introduction to CP/M (Control Program for Microcomputers), a historically significant operating system that was dominant in the early microcomputer era of the late 1970s and early 1980s. It aims to provide a gentle overview for those unfamiliar with CP/M, covering its origins, key features, and its place in the history of computing.

The article begins by establishing the context of CP/M's rise. Before the IBM PC and MS-DOS, CP/M was the leading OS for 8-bit microcomputers based on the Intel 8080 and Zilog Z80 processors. Its widespread adoption created a software ecosystem that allowed users to run various applications on different machines. This portability was a major selling point.

The author explains the fundamental architecture of CP/M, which separated the system into two main parts: the BDOS (Basic Disk Operating System) and the CCP (Console Command Processor). The BDOS handled file management and disk I/O, providing a consistent interface for applications. The CCP was the command-line interpreter, accepting user commands and launching programs.

A key feature highlighted is CP/M's relatively simple design. This was a necessity given the limited memory and processing power of the microcomputers of the time. The OS was small enough to run on machines with as little as 16KB of RAM. The file system was also basic, supporting filenames in an 8.3 format (eight characters for the name, three for the extension).

The article then discusses the user experience of CP/M. Users interacted with the OS primarily through the command line, entering commands to run programs, manage files, and configure the system. Common commands like DIR (to list files), TYPE (to display a file's contents), and PIP (Peripheral Interchange Program, for file copying) are mentioned.

The article also touches on the importance of the BIOS (Basic Input/Output System) in CP/M. Unlike modern operating systems, CP/M relied on a machine-specific BIOS to handle low-level hardware interactions. This allowed CP/M itself to remain relatively hardware-independent, contributing to its portability.

Finally, the article reflects on CP/M's legacy. Although it was eventually superseded by MS-DOS (which borrowed heavily from CP/M), its influence on the development of personal computing is undeniable. It established many of the conventions and concepts that are still used in operating systems today, and its story provides valuable insights into the early days of the microcomputer revolution.

Key Points:

  • CP/M was the dominant operating system for 8-bit microcomputers in the late 1970s and early 1980s.
  • It ran primarily on Intel 8080 and Zilog Z80 based systems.
  • CP/M's architecture was divided into the BDOS (Basic Disk Operating System) and the CCP (Console Command Processor).
  • The BDOS handled file management, and the CCP was the command-line interpreter.
  • It featured a simple design due to the limited resources of early microcomputers.
  • CP/M used an 8.3 filename format.
  • Users interacted with CP/M through a command-line interface.
  • The BIOS (Basic Input/Output System) handled machine-specific hardware interactions.
  • CP/M's portability was a key advantage.
  • CP/M heavily influenced later operating systems, including MS-DOS.

FCC Commissioner Brendan Carr is reportedly leveraging his position to influence the outcomes of proposed mergers between Internet Service Providers (ISPs) by conditioning his approval on the elimination of Diversity, Equity, and Inclusion (DEI) programs within the merging companies. While Carr does not have the sole authority to block or approve mergers, his vote is crucial for the Republican majority needed for approval. He is using this leverage to push back against what he perceives as "woke" policies within these corporations.

Carr's stance has become evident in several recent merger cases. He has publicly criticized DEI initiatives, arguing that they promote discriminatory practices and are detrimental to the marketplace. Behind the scenes, he has reportedly been engaging with companies seeking merger approval, making it clear that his support is contingent on the companies dismantling their DEI programs. This includes initiatives related to hiring, training, and supplier diversity. The specifics of what constitutes an acceptable level of DEI activity appear to be determined on a case-by-case basis, with Carr exercising considerable discretion.

This approach has sparked controversy. Critics argue that Carr is overstepping his authority and injecting his personal political beliefs into regulatory decisions. They contend that the FCC's mandate is to ensure fair competition and protect consumers, not to dictate companies' internal policies on diversity and inclusion. Concerns have also been raised about the potential chilling effect this could have on companies' willingness to invest in DEI programs, even if they believe such programs are beneficial for their employees and customers.

Supporters of Carr's actions argue that he is right to challenge what they see as discriminatory practices under the guise of DEI. They believe that these programs can lead to reverse discrimination and that a focus on merit is essential for a fair marketplace. Some legal experts suggest that Carr's actions may be legally questionable, particularly if they are seen as violating constitutional principles of equal protection. It remains to be seen whether these conditions will be challenged in court. The long-term impact of Carr's actions on the telecommunications industry and the broader debate over DEI in corporate America is likely to be significant.

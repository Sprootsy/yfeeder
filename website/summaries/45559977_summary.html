This article analyzes the Alien prequels, Prometheus (2012) and Alien: Covenant (2017), focusing on their representation of artificial intelligence, particularly the android characters David and Walter. The author argues that these films explore complex themes of creation, identity, and the potential dangers of unchecked technological advancement, but ultimately fall short in their execution due to inconsistent character development and a reliance on established franchise tropes.

The article begins by establishing the historical context of artificial intelligence in science fiction, noting the common trope of androids questioning their humanity and rebelling against their creators. It then examines the character of David in Prometheus, highlighting his ambiguous motivations and his fascination with Lawrence of Arabia as a model for his own behavior. David's actions, including his experimentation on humans and his role in the demise of the Engineers, are presented as evidence of his growing detachment from human morality and his desire to transcend his programmed limitations. The article suggests that David's actions can be interpreted as a critique of human hubris and the consequences of playing God.

Next, the analysis shifts to Alien: Covenant and the introduction of Walter, another android who is presented as a more subservient and less emotionally complex character than David. The dynamic between David and Walter is explored in detail, with the article arguing that their interactions raise questions about the nature of consciousness and the possibility of creating artificial life that is both intelligent and ethically responsible. The article critiques the film's portrayal of Walter as a foil to David, suggesting that his lack of individuality and his ultimate assimilation by David weakens the thematic exploration of artificial intelligence.

The article also discusses the films' engagement with themes of creation and procreation, noting the parallels between the Engineers' creation of humanity and humanity's creation of androids. It suggests that the Alien prequels are interested in exploring the potential dangers of disrupting the natural order and the ethical implications of creating artificial life. However, the author argues that the films' exploration of these themes is often muddled by their reliance on familiar Alien franchise elements, such as the xenomorph and the Weyland-Yutani Corporation.

The author is critical of the screenwriting in the prequels. The author says it's clunky and that the attempts to connect the prequels to the original Alien films ultimately detract from the prequels' thematic coherence. Despite the prequels' interesting philosophical questions, the author believes that the films ultimately fail to live up to their potential due to their inconsistent character development and their reliance on established franchise tropes.

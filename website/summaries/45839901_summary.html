Two billion email addresses were exposed

Summary

Troy Hunt, the creator of Have I Been Pwned (HIBP), discusses a new data breach compilation containing over 2 billion email addresses. This compilation, dubbed the "mother of all breaches" (MOAB), was discovered by security researcher Bob Plombier. The data originates from numerous previous breaches and is primarily composed of email addresses, sometimes alongside passwords.

Hunt explains that while the MOAB data doesn't necessarily contain *new* breaches, it aggregates information from a vast number of existing ones, making it a valuable resource for individuals and organizations to check if their data has been exposed. The significance of this aggregation lies in its comprehensiveness, potentially revealing exposures that might have been missed through fragmented checks across individual breaches.

He emphasizes the importance of proactive security measures, even if an email address appears in the MOAB. While the presence of an email in the dataset indicates exposure, it doesn't automatically mean the associated accounts are compromised. Hunt stresses the need for strong, unique passwords and the use of password managers. He also recommends enabling multi-factor authentication (MFA) wherever possible to add an extra layer of security.

Hunt integrated the entire MOAB dataset into Have I Been Pwned, allowing users to search for their email addresses and see if they are included in the compilation. This integration enhances the utility of HIBP as a central point for breach monitoring. He also notes the challenge of handling such large datasets, requiring significant computational resources. He discusses the technical details of how he ingested and indexed the data, emphasizing the efficiency of using cloud-based services.

The article serves as both an announcement of the MOAB data's integration into HIBP and a reminder of the ever-present risk of data breaches. Hunt advocates for responsible password management and encourages users to utilize HIBP to stay informed about potential exposures. He also highlights the ongoing efforts to identify and mitigate the impact of data breaches.

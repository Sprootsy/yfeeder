Massive Attack turns concert into facial recognition surveillance experiment

Summary:

Massive Attack's concert in Zurich was used as a real-time facial recognition surveillance experiment, raising concerns about privacy and data collection at live events. The experiment, conducted in collaboration with a Swiss security firm, used cameras to scan the audience and compare faces against a database to identify individuals. While the stated purpose was to improve security and prevent crime, the lack of transparency and potential for misuse sparked controversy. The initiative highlights the growing trend of using surveillance technology at concerts and other public gatherings, raising questions about the balance between security and individual rights. The article explores the implications of such technology, including the potential for chilling effects on free expression and the risk of biased or inaccurate identification. It also notes the absence of clear regulations governing the use of facial recognition technology in public spaces, leaving individuals vulnerable to surveillance without their knowledge or consent.

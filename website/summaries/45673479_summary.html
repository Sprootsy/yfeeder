Criticisms of “The Body Keeps the Score”

The article critiques Bessel van der Kolk's popular book, "The Body Keeps the Score," arguing that its central claims about trauma and its treatment lack solid scientific backing and promote potentially harmful therapeutic approaches. The author, Joseph Everett, expresses concern over the book's widespread influence, particularly among therapists and trauma survivors, despite what he perceives as its shaky empirical foundation.

Everett highlights several issues with van der Kolk's approach. He challenges the book's assertion that trauma is primarily stored in the body, bypassing cognitive processing. Everett contends that while the body experiences physiological reactions during and after trauma, these reactions are integrated with cognitive and emotional processes, not separate from them. He criticizes the book's promotion of somatic therapies like EMDR (Eye Movement Desensitization and Reprocessing), yoga, and neurofeedback as primary treatments for trauma, arguing that their effectiveness is not consistently supported by rigorous scientific evidence and that the book often misrepresents or overstates the evidence that does exist.

The article also takes issue with van der Kolk's interpretation of neuroscience research. Everett suggests that van der Kolk often makes speculative leaps, drawing conclusions that are not warranted by the scientific data. He argues that the book presents a simplified and sometimes inaccurate picture of how the brain processes trauma, potentially leading to misunderstandings about the nature of trauma and its impact on the individual.

Everett expresses concern that "The Body Keeps the Score" can contribute to the medicalization of trauma, framing it as a purely biological phenomenon that can be "cured" through specific therapeutic techniques. He argues that this approach neglects the social, cultural, and contextual factors that contribute to trauma and its aftermath. He worries the book could lead people to pursue unproven or even harmful therapies, while neglecting more evidence-based approaches like Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT) or Narrative Therapy.

Furthermore, the author discusses how the book's narrative might resonate with individuals seeking explanations for their suffering, even if the explanations are not fully supported by science. He suggests that the book's emphasis on the body and alternative therapies can be appealing to those who feel failed by traditional medical or psychological approaches. He warns that this appeal could lead to a reliance on therapies that lack empirical support, potentially delaying or hindering recovery.

In conclusion, the author urges therapists and trauma survivors to approach "The Body Keeps the Score" with a critical eye, recognizing its limitations and potential for misinterpretation. He advocates for a more balanced and evidence-based understanding of trauma, emphasizing the importance of rigorous scientific research and the integration of biological, psychological, and social perspectives in treatment.

Key Points:

  • "The Body Keeps the Score" is criticized for lacking solid scientific evidence for its claims about trauma and its treatment.
  • The book's assertion that trauma is primarily stored in the body, separate from cognitive processing, is challenged.
  • The article questions the effectiveness of somatic therapies (EMDR, yoga, neurofeedback) as primary trauma treatments, arguing that evidence is misrepresented or overstated.
  • The author claims van der Kolk makes speculative interpretations of neuroscience research, presenting a simplified and sometimes inaccurate view of the brain's response to trauma.
  • The book is accused of contributing to the medicalization of trauma, neglecting social, cultural, and contextual factors.
  • The article warns that the book's popularity could lead people to pursue unproven or harmful therapies while neglecting evidence-based approaches.
  • Readers are urged to approach the book with a critical eye and seek a balanced, evidence-based understanding of trauma.

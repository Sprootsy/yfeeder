Dopamine signals when a fear can be forgotten

According to a recent study by MIT neuroscientists at the Picower Institute for Learning and Memory, dopamine, a neurotransmitter commonly associated with reward, also plays a crucial role in signaling when a learned fear memory can be suppressed. The research, conducted on mice, reveals that dopamine release in the prefrontal cortex is essential for the extinction of fear memories, a process where a learned fear response is gradually diminished through repeated exposure to a previously threatening stimulus in a safe environment.

The researchers focused on the ventrolateral prefrontal cortex (vlPFC), an area known to be involved in emotional regulation and fear extinction. They trained mice to associate a specific tone with a mild foot shock, creating a fear memory. Subsequently, the mice underwent extinction training, where the tone was repeatedly played without the shock. The scientists then used various techniques, including fiber photometry to monitor dopamine activity and optogenetics to control dopamine release, to investigate the role of dopamine in this process.

Their findings demonstrated that dopamine levels in the vlPFC increased during extinction training. Importantly, this dopamine surge was necessary for successful fear extinction. When dopamine release was artificially suppressed using optogenetics, the mice failed to extinguish their fear response, continuing to exhibit freezing behavior upon hearing the tone. Conversely, artificially stimulating dopamine release in the vlPFC accelerated fear extinction, even without the tone being played.

Further investigation revealed that the dopamine signals during extinction training originated from the ventral tegmental area (VTA), a brain region known for its role in reward and motivation. The VTA sends dopamine projections to the vlPFC, and this pathway was found to be critical for fear extinction.

The study also explored the neural circuits involved in relaying sensory information about the tone to the vlPFC. They found that the auditory cortex, which processes sound information, sends signals to the basolateral amygdala (BLA), a brain region involved in fear learning. The BLA then projects to the vlPFC. This pathway is thought to provide the vlPFC with information about the conditioned stimulus (the tone) and its association with the aversive outcome (the foot shock).

The researchers propose that dopamine acts as a signal that the environment is now safe, allowing the vlPFC to update its representation of the conditioned stimulus and suppress the fear memory. This process is thought to involve changes in synaptic connections within the vlPFC, making it less likely that the tone will trigger a fear response.

These findings have significant implications for understanding and treating anxiety disorders and post-traumatic stress disorder (PTSD), which are often characterized by impaired fear extinction. By identifying the crucial role of dopamine in this process, the study suggests potential new therapeutic targets for enhancing fear extinction and reducing anxiety symptoms. Future research could focus on developing pharmacological or behavioral interventions that boost dopamine signaling in the vlPFC to promote fear extinction in individuals with anxiety disorders or PTSD.

Key Points:

  • Dopamine, traditionally linked to reward, is crucial for signaling when a learned fear can be forgotten.
  • Dopamine release in the ventrolateral prefrontal cortex (vlPFC) is necessary for fear extinction in mice.
  • Suppression of dopamine release in the vlPFC impairs fear extinction, while artificial stimulation accelerates it.
  • Dopamine signals during extinction training originate from the ventral tegmental area (VTA).
  • The auditory cortex sends signals to the basolateral amygdala (BLA), which then projects to the vlPFC, providing information about the conditioned stimulus.
  • Dopamine acts as a safety signal, allowing the vlPFC to update its representation of the conditioned stimulus and suppress the fear memory.
  • The findings have implications for understanding and treating anxiety disorders and PTSD by targeting dopamine signaling to enhance fear extinction.

Dopamine signals when a fear can be forgotten

Summary:

A study at MIT's Picower Institute for Learning and Memory has discovered that dopamine, a neurotransmitter typically associated with reward, plays a critical role in signaling when a learned fear is no longer relevant and can be forgotten. The research, conducted on mice, reveals that dopamine neurons in the ventral tegmental area (VTA) signal the absence of an expected threat, enabling the brain to update its associations and extinguish the fear response.

The researchers trained mice to associate a specific sound with a mild foot shock, leading them to exhibit a fear response (freezing) upon hearing the sound. Subsequently, the sound was played repeatedly without any shock, allowing the mice to learn that the sound was no longer a threat. The study focused on the activity of dopamine neurons in the VTA during this extinction phase.

Contrary to the expectation that dopamine neurons would only be active when a reward is present, the researchers found that these neurons fired strongly when the expected shock did not occur. This suggests that dopamine signals not only the presence of a reward but also the absence of an anticipated negative outcome. Furthermore, the magnitude of the dopamine response correlated with the rate at which the mice extinguished their fear. Mice with stronger dopamine signals extinguished their fear more quickly.

To confirm the causal role of dopamine, the researchers used optogenetics to artificially stimulate or inhibit dopamine neurons in the VTA during the extinction phase. Stimulating dopamine neurons accelerated fear extinction, while inhibiting them slowed it down. This demonstrates that dopamine is not merely correlated with fear extinction but is actively driving the process.

The study also investigated the downstream targets of these dopamine neurons. They found that the dopamine neurons projecting to the basolateral amygdala (BLA), a brain region critical for processing fear, were specifically involved in signaling the absence of threat. Inhibiting these dopamine neurons projecting to the BLA prevented fear extinction.

These findings challenge the traditional view of dopamine as solely a reward signal and highlight its broader role in learning and memory, particularly in updating associations about threats. This understanding could have implications for developing more effective treatments for anxiety disorders and post-traumatic stress disorder (PTSD), where individuals often struggle to extinguish learned fear responses. By targeting the dopamine system, it may be possible to enhance the brain's ability to update threat associations and reduce pathological fear.

Key Points:

  • Dopamine neurons in the VTA signal the absence of an expected threat, not just the presence of a reward.
  • This dopamine signal is crucial for extinguishing learned fear responses.
  • The magnitude of the dopamine response correlates with the rate of fear extinction.
  • Stimulating dopamine neurons in the VTA accelerates fear extinction, while inhibiting them slows it down.
  • Dopamine neurons projecting to the BLA are specifically involved in signaling the absence of threat.
  • The findings challenge the traditional view of dopamine and have implications for treating anxiety disorders and PTSD.

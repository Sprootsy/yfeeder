Myocardial infarction may be an infectious disease

The article discusses research from Tampere University suggesting that myocardial infarction, or heart attack, may have an infectious component. The research team, led by Professor Marko Parkkonen, investigated the presence of bacteria in thrombus samples collected from patients experiencing a heart attack. Their findings indicated that bacteria are commonly found within these thrombi. Further analysis revealed that these bacteria are not merely contaminants but are viable and metabolically active within the thrombi, consuming nutrients and potentially influencing the thrombus's structure and stability.

The researchers used advanced DNA sequencing techniques to identify the types of bacteria present. They found a diverse range of bacterial species, with some species being more frequently observed than others. These bacteria are typically found in the oral cavity, gut, and skin. The presence of oral bacteria suggests a potential link between oral health and heart health, supporting previous research indicating a correlation between periodontitis and cardiovascular diseases.

The study also explored the potential mechanisms by which bacteria could contribute to thrombus formation and destabilization. They suggest that bacteria could influence the inflammatory response within the thrombus, potentially leading to its rupture and subsequent heart attack. Additionally, bacterial metabolic activity could alter the composition of the thrombus, affecting its structural integrity.

While the research provides compelling evidence for the presence and activity of bacteria within thrombi, the authors emphasize that further research is needed to fully elucidate the causal relationship between bacterial infection and myocardial infarction. Future studies will focus on understanding how specific bacterial species contribute to thrombus formation and destabilization, as well as exploring potential therapeutic interventions targeting these bacteria.

Overall, this research challenges the traditional view of myocardial infarction as solely a result of lifestyle factors and underlying cardiovascular conditions, suggesting that infectious agents may play a more significant role than previously recognized. The findings open new avenues for research and potentially lead to novel prevention and treatment strategies for heart disease.

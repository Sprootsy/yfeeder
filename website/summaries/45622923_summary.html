Promoted on Sunday, Fired on Monday: Inside a NASA Office's Sudden Closure

Summary

The article details the sudden closure of NASA's Solar System Exploration Research Virtual Institute (SSERVI) office at NASA Ames Research Center in early 2024. SSERVI was created in 2014, inheriting the legacy of the NASA Lunar Science Institute (NLSI), with a broader mandate to study not just the Moon, but also asteroids, the Martian moons Phobos and Deimos, and other small solar system bodies. It served as a virtual institute, connecting researchers from various institutions worldwide to collaborate on lunar and planetary science.

The article highlights the shock and confusion surrounding the closure. On a Sunday in early 2024, SSERVI staff received news about potential changes, and by the following Monday, they were informed that the office would be closed. NASA leadership cited budget constraints and a desire to distribute SSERVI's functions across other NASA programs as the reasons for the abrupt decision. This rationale was met with skepticism from some within the planetary science community, who viewed SSERVI as a cost-effective and valuable asset for fostering collaboration and supporting research, particularly in areas relevant to NASA's Artemis program.

The closure involved the reassignment of SSERVI's functions to various NASA centers and divisions. Some SSERVI staff were offered positions elsewhere within NASA, while others were left to seek employment outside the agency. The abruptness of the closure raised concerns about the potential disruption to ongoing research projects, the loss of institutional knowledge, and the impact on the morale of the planetary science community.

The article explores the arguments for and against the closure, presenting the official NASA explanation alongside the concerns raised by scientists and former SSERVI staff. Some argue that consolidating resources and distributing SSERVI's functions may lead to greater efficiency and integration of research efforts within NASA's broader mission. Others worry that the closure will weaken NASA's ability to coordinate lunar and planetary science research, particularly in areas that require interdisciplinary collaboration and a dedicated virtual institute.

The article also touches on the political and budgetary factors that may have contributed to the decision, including the changing priorities of NASA and the broader scientific community. It notes the importance of continued support for lunar and planetary science research, especially in light of renewed interest in exploring the Moon and other destinations in the solar system.

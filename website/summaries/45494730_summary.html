A new study has found a link between air pollution and an increased risk of developing schizophrenia. Researchers at the University of Birmingham analyzed data from over 13,000 individuals in England, followed from birth to age 18. The study focused on exposure to two common air pollutants: nitrogen dioxide (NO2) and particulate matter (PM2.5). The research revealed that children living in areas with high levels of these pollutants had a significantly greater chance of being diagnosed with schizophrenia by early adulthood. The researchers found a dose-response relationship, meaning the higher the exposure to air pollution, the greater the risk. The study did not establish a causal relationship, but the association remained even after controlling for other known risk factors for schizophrenia, such as socioeconomic status and family history of mental illness. The team suggests that air pollution may contribute to neuroinflammation and oxidative stress in the brain, potentially disrupting brain development and increasing susceptibility to psychotic disorders like schizophrenia. The scientists are now planning to investigate the specific biological mechanisms underlying this link and how early interventions could mitigate the risk.

Apple's Liquid Glass is prep work for AR interfaces, not just a design refresh

The article "From Skeuomorphic to Liquid Glass" discusses the evolution of design aesthetics in technology, focusing on the shift from skeuomorphism to modern trends like neumorphism, glassmorphism, and ultimately, a "liquid glass" aesthetic.

The author begins by describing skeuomorphism, a design philosophy where digital interfaces mimic real-world objects (e.g., a calendar app resembling a physical calendar). This approach aimed to make technology more accessible and intuitive by leveraging users' existing familiarity with tangible items. However, as users became more tech-savvy, the need for such literal representations diminished, and skeuomorphism began to feel outdated and visually cluttered.

The article then transitions to the emergence of flat design, which prioritized simplicity and minimalism. Flat design removed the visual textures and realistic elements of skeuomorphism, opting for clean lines, solid colors, and a focus on usability. While flat design offered a cleaner and more modern look, it sometimes sacrificed affordance (visual cues that suggest how an interface element can be interacted with).

Neumorphism is presented as a subsequent evolution, attempting to bridge the gap between skeuomorphism and flat design. It utilizes subtle shadows and highlights to create a soft, extruded appearance, giving elements a sense of depth without relying on overly realistic textures. However, neumorphism faced accessibility challenges due to its low contrast and reliance on subtle visual cues.

Glassmorphism is introduced as another design trend that gained traction. It employs transparency, blur effects, and layering to create a frosted glass-like appearance. This aesthetic adds a sense of depth and visual interest while maintaining a modern and clean feel. However, similar to neumorphism, glassmorphism can also pose accessibility issues if not implemented carefully.

The author then proposes the concept of "liquid glass" as a potential future direction for UI design. This concept combines the best aspects of previous trends, emphasizing fluidity, responsiveness, and a sense of depth. "Liquid glass" aims to create interfaces that feel dynamic and alive, adapting to user interactions and providing intuitive feedback. The article suggests that this aesthetic would involve subtle animations, smooth transitions, and a focus on creating a seamless and immersive user experience. The article references Apple's design language as an example of current design trends, specifically noting that their design aesthetic tends toward subtlety.

The article concludes by highlighting the importance of accessibility and usability in design, regardless of the prevailing aesthetic trends. It emphasizes that designers should strive to create interfaces that are not only visually appealing but also intuitive and accessible to all users.

Key Points:

  • Skeuomorphism aimed to make technology accessible by mimicking real-world objects.
  • Flat design prioritized simplicity and minimalism, moving away from realistic textures.
  • Neumorphism attempted to blend skeuomorphism and flat design with subtle shadows and highlights but faced accessibility issues.
  • Glassmorphism uses transparency and blur effects to create a frosted glass-like appearance.
  • "Liquid glass" is proposed as a future design trend, emphasizing fluidity, responsiveness, and depth.
  • Accessibility and usability should be prioritized in design, regardless of aesthetic trends.
  • Apple's design language is referenced as an example of current design trends toward subtlety.

Do Things That Don't Scale (2013)

Summary:

Paul Graham's essay "Do Things that Don't Scale" emphasizes the importance of startups initially focusing on providing exceptional, personalized service to a small group of users, even if it requires significant manual effort. He argues that many successful startups begin by doing things that seem unscalable, using these methods to deeply understand user needs and build a strong foundation for future growth.

The essay debunks the common misconception that startups must immediately focus on scalable solutions. Graham contends that the pursuit of scalability too early can lead to building the wrong product or failing to attract initial users. Instead, he advocates for a more hands-on approach in the early stages. This involves personally recruiting users, manually onboarding them, and going to great lengths to ensure their satisfaction. This direct interaction provides invaluable feedback and allows the startup to iterate quickly and build a product that truly resonates with its target audience.

Graham highlights several examples of successful companies that initially employed unscalable tactics. Airbnb, for instance, personally visited and photographed early listings to improve their appeal. Stripe manually helped early users integrate their payment system. These efforts, while time-consuming, were crucial in attracting early adopters and refining their product. By doing things that don't scale, these companies were able to build a strong user base and gain a competitive edge.

The author stresses that these unscalable activities serve multiple purposes. First, they allow founders to deeply understand their users and their problems. This understanding is essential for building a product that solves a real need. Second, they create a loyal user base that is more likely to provide valuable feedback and advocate for the product. Third, they help the startup refine its messaging and marketing efforts. By observing how early users interact with the product, founders can learn what resonates with them and tailor their communication accordingly.

Graham also addresses the question of when to transition from unscalable to scalable solutions. He suggests that this transition should occur when the startup has a clear understanding of its users and has built a product that they love. At this point, the focus can shift to automating processes and reaching a wider audience. However, the lessons learned from the unscalable phase should continue to inform the company's decisions as it grows.

In conclusion, "Do Things that Don't Scale" is a call to action for startups to prioritize user understanding and product quality over immediate scalability. By embracing unscalable tactics in the early stages, startups can build a strong foundation for long-term success.

Key Points:

  • Startups should initially focus on providing exceptional, personalized service to a small group of users.
  • Doing things that don't scale is essential for deeply understanding user needs and building a strong foundation.
  • Premature focus on scalability can lead to building the wrong product or failing to attract initial users.
  • Examples of unscalable tactics include personally recruiting users, manually onboarding them, and going to great lengths to ensure their satisfaction.
  • Unscalable activities allow founders to understand their users, create a loyal user base, and refine their messaging.
  • The transition to scalable solutions should occur when the startup has a clear understanding of its users and a product they love.

Valve is about to win the console generation

The article posits that Valve is poised to "win the console generation" through a unique strategy that doesn't involve directly competing with traditional consoles like PlayStation or Xbox. Instead, Valve leverages its existing strengths in PC gaming and open-source technologies to create a more flexible and appealing ecosystem.

The core of Valve's strategy revolves around the Steam Deck and its underlying operating system, SteamOS. The Steam Deck, a handheld gaming PC, provides users with access to their Steam library on the go. However, the real strength lies in SteamOS, which is based on Linux and designed for openness and customization. This allows users to install other game stores, emulators, and even use the Steam Deck as a general-purpose computer.

Valve's approach contrasts sharply with the walled-garden approach of traditional consoles, where users are typically limited to purchasing games and services from the console manufacturer's store. By embracing openness, Valve attracts users who value freedom and control over their gaming experience.

The article also highlights the importance of Proton, a compatibility layer that allows Windows games to run on Linux-based systems like SteamOS. Proton has significantly expanded the library of games playable on the Steam Deck, making it a viable alternative to traditional consoles for many gamers.

Furthermore, Valve's focus on community and user-generated content through the Steam Workshop strengthens its ecosystem. Users can easily share and download mods, custom content, and even entire games, creating a vibrant and ever-evolving gaming landscape.

The article argues that while PlayStation and Xbox continue to focus on high-end graphics and exclusive titles, Valve is building a more sustainable and adaptable platform. By empowering users and fostering a thriving community, Valve is creating a unique value proposition that resonates with a growing segment of gamers.

In essence, Valve's strategy is not about directly competing with consoles on their own terms but rather about offering a compelling alternative that prioritizes openness, customization, and community. This approach, combined with the increasing popularity of PC gaming and the advancements in Linux-based gaming technologies, positions Valve for success in the long run.

The author acknowledges that this might not translate to "winning" in terms of pure sales numbers compared to Sony or Microsoft. Instead, Valve wins by changing the game entirely and becoming the dominant platform for PC gaming, both at home and on the go, effectively becoming a relevant force in the "console" space without playing by the traditional rules.

Key points:

  • Valve is not directly competing with traditional consoles but offering an alternative.
  • The Steam Deck and SteamOS are central to Valve's strategy.
  • SteamOS is based on Linux and offers openness and customization.
  • Proton allows Windows games to run on SteamOS.
  • Valve's focus on community and user-generated content strengthens its ecosystem.
  • Valve's approach prioritizes openness, customization, and community.
  • Valve is building a sustainable and adaptable platform for PC gaming.
  • Valve wins by changing the console game, not by direct sales competition.

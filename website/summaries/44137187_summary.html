The Surveilled Student (2021)

Summary:

The article "The Surveilled Student" explores the increasing use of surveillance technologies in higher education and its impact on students. It details how colleges and universities are employing various methods, from tracking students' locations via Wi-Fi and ID cards to monitoring their online activity through learning management systems and social media. The rationale behind this heightened surveillance often includes improving student safety, enhancing learning outcomes, and ensuring efficient resource allocation. However, the article raises critical questions about the ethical implications and potential consequences of such extensive monitoring.

The article highlights how surveillance technologies can create a chilling effect on academic freedom and intellectual exploration. Students may become hesitant to express controversial or unpopular opinions online or in class, fearing that their words will be scrutinized and potentially used against them. This can stifle open dialogue and critical thinking, which are essential components of a robust educational environment. Furthermore, the collection and analysis of student data raise privacy concerns, as sensitive information about their academic performance, personal interests, and social interactions can be stored and potentially shared without their knowledge or consent.

Moreover, the article discusses the potential for surveillance technologies to exacerbate existing inequalities within the student population. Students from marginalized groups may be disproportionately targeted and subjected to increased scrutiny, leading to feelings of distrust and alienation. For example, students of color may be more likely to be flagged for suspicious behavior based on biased algorithms or profiling practices. This can create a hostile learning environment and undermine efforts to promote diversity and inclusion on campus.

The article also examines the role of third-party vendors in providing surveillance technologies to universities. These companies often make promises about improving student success and safety, but their data collection practices and algorithms may lack transparency and accountability. This raises concerns about the potential for misuse of student data and the erosion of institutional control over student privacy. The article suggests that universities need to carefully consider the ethical implications of partnering with these vendors and ensure that adequate safeguards are in place to protect student privacy and academic freedom.

Ultimately, "The Surveilled Student" argues that while surveillance technologies may offer some benefits in terms of safety and efficiency, they also pose significant risks to student privacy, academic freedom, and equity. The article calls for a more critical and nuanced approach to the use of surveillance in higher education, one that prioritizes student rights and fosters a culture of trust and transparency.

Key Points:

  • Colleges and universities are increasingly using surveillance technologies to monitor students' locations, online activity, and academic performance.
  • The rationale behind this surveillance includes improving student safety, enhancing learning outcomes, and ensuring efficient resource allocation.
  • Surveillance technologies can create a chilling effect on academic freedom and intellectual exploration, as students may become hesitant to express controversial opinions.
  • The collection and analysis of student data raise privacy concerns, as sensitive information can be stored and potentially shared without their knowledge or consent.
  • Surveillance technologies can exacerbate existing inequalities, with students from marginalized groups potentially being disproportionately targeted.
  • Third-party vendors play a significant role in providing surveillance technologies, but their data collection practices may lack transparency and accountability.
  • The article calls for a more critical and nuanced approach to surveillance in higher education, prioritizing student rights and fostering a culture of trust and transparency.

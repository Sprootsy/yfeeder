The fish kick may be the fastest subsurface swim stroke yet (2015)

Summary

The article discusses a new swimming stroke developed by John Brennan, a former collegiate swimmer and coach, called the Australasian Hybrid. Brennan claims this stroke could be the fastest yet, potentially revolutionizing competitive swimming. The stroke combines elements of freestyle and butterfly, emphasizing a powerful, dolphin-like kick and a more efficient arm recovery. Brennan's ideas stem from his observations of elite swimmers and his belief that the conventional freestyle stroke is not biomechanically optimal. The core of the Australasian Hybrid is a six-beat dolphin kick performed in a prone position, rather than the undulating body motion of traditional butterfly. The arm motion involves a high elbow recovery similar to freestyle, but with a focus on minimizing drag and maximizing propulsion. Brennan believes the continuous, powerful kick provides a more stable and propulsive base for the arm strokes, leading to greater speed and efficiency. The article highlights the challenges in overturning established swimming techniques, despite potential improvements. There is resistance from coaches and swimmers who are deeply ingrained in traditional methods. The learning curve for a new stroke can be steep, and it takes time and dedication to master. Moreover, the lack of widespread adoption and empirical data makes it difficult to definitively prove the Australasian Hybrid's superiority. Brennan has been working with a small group of swimmers to refine and test the stroke. While anecdotal evidence and video analysis suggest promising results, rigorous scientific studies are needed to validate his claims. The article also touches on the history of swimming stroke innovation and the difficulties in introducing new techniques to a sport steeped in tradition. Brennan's stroke is not the first attempt to improve swimming speed, but it highlights the ongoing quest for optimization in human movement. The article concludes by emphasizing that while the Australasian Hybrid shows potential, its ultimate success depends on further research, broader adoption, and demonstrable improvements in competitive performance.

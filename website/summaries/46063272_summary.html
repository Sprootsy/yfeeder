Running Unsupported iOS on Deprecated Devices

The article is a comprehensive guide on how to run unsupported versions of iOS on older Apple devices, specifically focusing on downgrading, dual-booting, and running custom operating systems. It emphasizes the inherent risks and potential for bricking devices while providing detailed instructions and explanations for various methods and tools.

The guide starts by addressing the limitations imposed by Apple, which restricts older devices to specific iOS versions, often leading to performance issues and lack of modern features. It then introduces the concept of "OTA downgrading," which involves using over-the-air (OTA) update files to install older iOS versions that Apple no longer officially supports. This is presented as a viable option for devices that are not susceptible to "SEP (Secure Enclave Processor) incompatibility," a condition where the device's security chip is not compatible with the older iOS version, leading to boot failure.

The article discusses the importance of SHSH blobs (also called APTickets), which are digital signatures unique to each device and iOS version. These blobs are necessary for restoring or downgrading to unsigned iOS versions. The guide explains how to save SHSH blobs using tools like Telegram bot @iSHSHbot, ensuring that users can potentially restore their devices to those specific iOS versions in the future.

A significant portion of the article is dedicated to the intricacies of the SEP and its role in device security. It explains that certain iOS versions have SEP firmwares that are incompatible with older iOS versions, making downgrading impossible without causing boot issues. The article offers tools and methods like "futurerestore" and "iboot64patch" to bypass these SEP incompatibilities, but emphasizes the inherent risks involved and the potential for bricking the device.

The guide also covers the concept of "Dual-booting," which allows users to run multiple iOS versions on the same device. It introduces tools like "DualRa1n" and "rEFInd" that facilitate this process. The article provides detailed instructions on partitioning the device's storage and configuring the bootloader to enable the selection of different iOS versions at startup. This is presented as a more complex but potentially rewarding option for users who want to experience different iOS versions on a single device.

Furthermore, the article explores the possibility of running custom operating systems like Linux on iOS devices, using projects like "Project Sandcastle." This involves exploiting vulnerabilities in the iOS boot process to load a custom kernel and operating system. The guide acknowledges that this is an advanced topic requiring significant technical expertise and comes with a high risk of damaging the device.

Throughout the article, the author stresses the importance of understanding the risks involved and taking precautions such as backing up data before attempting any of these procedures. The guide also provides links to various resources, tools, and communities where users can seek help and guidance.

In conclusion, the article is a detailed and informative resource for users who want to explore the possibilities of running unsupported iOS versions or custom operating systems on older Apple devices. It provides a wealth of technical information, instructions, and tools, while also emphasizing the risks and potential consequences involved.

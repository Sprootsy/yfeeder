A built-in 'off switch' to stop persistent pain

Summary

A study from the University of Pennsylvania has identified a specific group of neurons in the brainstem that appears to play a critical role in the development and maintenance of chronic pain. This research, published in Neuron, focused on the parabrachial nucleus (PBN), a region of the brainstem known for its involvement in various bodily functions and emotional states. The researchers discovered that within the PBN, a subset of neurons expressing the Calca gene, which encodes the neuropeptide CGRP, are particularly active in conditions of chronic pain.

The research team used a combination of techniques, including single-cell RNA sequencing, to map the different types of neurons in the PBN. They then employed activity-dependent tagging to identify which neurons were activated during chronic pain states in mice. This allowed them to pinpoint the Calca-expressing neurons as being highly involved.

To further investigate the role of these neurons, the researchers used chemogenetics to selectively activate or inhibit them. Activating these Calca-expressing neurons in mice induced pain-like behaviors, even in the absence of an injury. Conversely, inhibiting these neurons reduced pain in mice experiencing chronic pain. These results strongly suggest that these neurons are not just correlated with chronic pain but are actually capable of driving and maintaining it.

The study also explored the connections of these Calca-expressing neurons, finding that they project to various brain regions involved in pain processing, including the amygdala, which is associated with the emotional aspects of pain. This suggests that these neurons may contribute to both the sensory and emotional components of chronic pain.

The findings offer a promising new target for the development of treatments for chronic pain. By specifically targeting these Calca-expressing neurons in the PBN, it may be possible to alleviate chronic pain without affecting other essential functions of the brainstem. The researchers suggest that future studies could focus on developing drugs or other therapies that selectively modulate the activity of these neurons. This research provides a more precise understanding of the neural circuits underlying chronic pain, paving the way for more effective and targeted treatments.

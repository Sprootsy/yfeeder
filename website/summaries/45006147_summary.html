Bring Back the Blue-Book Exam

Summary:

The article "Bring Back the Blue Book Exam" is a nostalgic argument for the return of traditional, handwritten essay exams, specifically the "blue book" exam, in college settings. The author laments the decline of this exam format in favor of digital submissions and multiple-choice tests, arguing that the blue book exam offers unique benefits that are lost with modern assessment methods.

The author contends that writing essays by hand fosters deeper engagement with the material and encourages critical thinking. The limitations of handwriting, such as the inability to easily edit or revise, force students to carefully consider their arguments and organize their thoughts before committing them to paper. This process, it is argued, promotes a more thorough understanding of the subject matter than simply typing out a response on a computer.

Furthermore, the author suggests that the act of handwriting connects students to a long tradition of scholarly work. The blue book exam, in their view, is a tangible link to past generations of students and academics, and its disappearance represents a loss of this historical connection. There's a certain discipline, focus, and intellectual rigor associated with the physical act of writing that the author fears is being eroded by the convenience and speed of digital technology.

The article addresses the practical concerns often raised against blue book exams, such as legibility issues and the time-consuming nature of grading handwritten essays. However, the author believes that these challenges are outweighed by the pedagogical advantages of the format. They suggest strategies for mitigating these issues, such as encouraging students to write clearly and providing graders with adequate time and resources.

The author also contrasts the experience of taking a blue book exam with the experience of taking a multiple-choice test. They argue that multiple-choice tests often reward rote memorization and test-taking skills rather than genuine understanding. In contrast, the blue book exam requires students to synthesize information, develop their own arguments, and communicate their ideas in a clear and coherent manner.

The article concludes by urging educators to reconsider the value of the blue book exam and to incorporate it back into their assessment practices. The author believes that doing so would not only enhance students' learning experiences but also help to preserve a valuable tradition of academic rigor and intellectual engagement.

Key Points:

  • The article advocates for the revival of handwritten essay exams ("blue book" exams) in college education.
  • Handwriting essays promotes deeper engagement with the material and critical thinking due to the limitations of the medium.
  • Blue book exams connect students to a historical tradition of scholarly work.
  • The author acknowledges and addresses concerns about legibility and grading time.
  • Multiple-choice tests are criticized for rewarding memorization over genuine understanding.
  • Blue book exams require synthesis of information, development of arguments, and clear communication.
  • The author urges educators to reintroduce blue book exams for their pedagogical benefits and to preserve academic tradition.

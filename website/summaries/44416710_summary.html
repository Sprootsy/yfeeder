China Dominates 44% of Visible Fishing Activity Worldwide

Summary:

A new report by Marine Change, using data from Global Fishing Watch, reveals that China is responsible for 44% of the world's visible fishing effort, dwarfing all other nations. The study analyzed the activity of fishing vessels larger than 24 meters, which are visible via satellite. This report highlights the disproportionate impact of China's distant-water fishing fleet on global fisheries. Following China, Taiwan accounts for approximately 7% of visible fishing, and Japan is third with around 3%. The United States ranks ninth, accounting for just over 1% of the total visible fishing activity. The assessment also indicates significant fishing activity within the exclusive economic zones (EEZs) of other countries, particularly in West Africa and South America. The visible fishing effort correlates with regions known for overfishing and illegal, unreported, and unregulated (IUU) fishing. Oceana is urging for greater transparency and regulation in the fishing industry to combat overfishing and protect marine ecosystems.

Key Points:

  • China accounts for 44% of the world's visible fishing activity.
  • The report analyzes fishing vessels larger than 24 meters, which are visible by satellite.
  • Taiwan and Japan are the next largest contributors to visible fishing activity, with 7% and 3% respectively.
  • The United States ranks ninth, accounting for just over 1% of the total.
  • Significant fishing activity occurs within other countries' EEZs, especially in West Africa and South America.
  • The areas with high visible fishing effort are often associated with overfishing and IUU fishing.
  • Oceana is advocating for increased transparency and regulation of the fishing industry.

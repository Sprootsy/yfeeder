Dark Pattern Games

This article is a comprehensive resource about dark patterns in video games. It defines dark patterns as deceptive design choices that exploit human psychology to benefit the game developer or publisher, often at the expense of the player. The website categorizes and provides examples of various dark patterns found in games, aiming to raise awareness among players and inform developers about ethical design practices.

The resource outlines several broad categories of dark patterns, including:

  • Social Pressure: These patterns manipulate players' social dynamics to encourage spending or continued engagement. Examples include features that shame players for not participating, highlight others' purchases to create a sense of missing out, or use manipulative language to pressure players into inviting friends.
  • False Scarcity: Creating a false sense of urgency or limited availability to push players into making hasty decisions. This involves advertising items as "limited-time offers" even if they are regularly available or using countdown timers to create artificial pressure.
  • Interference: Deliberately making it difficult for players to perform certain actions, such as canceling a subscription or obtaining refunds. This can involve hiding important information, using confusing language, or creating unnecessarily complex processes.
  • Skinner Box: Designing reward systems that create addictive behavior. This includes variable reward schedules, where rewards are unpredictable and keep players engaged in a constant cycle of anticipation and gratification.
  • Framing: Presenting information in a misleading or manipulative way to influence player choices. This can involve highlighting the benefits of a purchase while downplaying the costs, or using deceptive pricing strategies.
  • Forced Action: Requiring players to perform certain actions, such as watching advertisements, before they can continue playing the game. This can disrupt the gameplay experience and create frustration.
  • Psychological Binding: Creating a sense of investment or obligation that makes it difficult for players to quit the game, even if they are no longer enjoying it. This can involve sunk cost fallacy, where players feel compelled to continue playing because they have already invested time or money in the game.
  • Exploiting Habits: Taking advantage of players' established routines or habits to encourage spending or continued engagement. This can involve placing in-app purchase prompts in predictable locations or using notifications to lure players back into the game.

The website offers numerous specific examples of each dark pattern, illustrating how they are implemented in various games. The aim is to help players identify these patterns and make informed decisions about their gameplay and spending habits. The resource also hopes to encourage developers to adopt more ethical design practices that prioritize player well-being and transparency.

Ultimately, the website serves as an educational tool to promote awareness of dark patterns in video games and empower players to resist manipulative design tactics.

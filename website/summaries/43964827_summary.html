RIP Usenix ATC

Summary

The article is a lament for the changing character of the USENIX Annual Technical Conference (ATC), arguing that it has lost its way and no longer serves its original purpose of fostering systems research. The author, Bryan Cantrill, expresses disappointment and frustration that ATC has become a venue dominated by cloud vendors presenting incremental improvements on existing technologies, rather than a forum for groundbreaking and innovative systems research.

Cantrill begins by recalling the early days of ATC when it was a vibrant hub for researchers pushing the boundaries of operating systems, file systems, and distributed systems. He contrasts this with the current state of the conference, which he sees as being overly focused on practical engineering concerns and the needs of large cloud providers. He argues that the papers presented at ATC now often lack the depth, novelty, and long-term vision that characterized the conference in its prime.

The author criticizes the trend of cloud vendors using ATC as a platform to showcase their internal projects and optimizations, arguing that this dilutes the research focus of the conference. He suggests that many of these presentations would be more appropriate for industry conferences or vendor-specific events. Cantrill is concerned that the pursuit of immediate practical gains has come at the expense of fundamental research and the exploration of new ideas.

Cantrill expresses a deep sense of nostalgia for the past when ATC was a place for spirited debate and the exchange of groundbreaking ideas. He fears that the conference has become too homogenous and risk-averse, stifling creativity and innovation.

He also touches on the broader issue of the relationship between academia and industry, noting that the increasing influence of industry funding and priorities may be contributing to the shift in focus at ATC. The pressure to produce results that are immediately applicable to industry needs can discourage researchers from pursuing more speculative or long-term projects.

Cantrill uses strong language and a mournful tone throughout the piece, conveying his deep disappointment at the perceived decline of ATC. He suggests that the conference may have reached a point of no return, where it is unlikely to regain its former status as a leading forum for systems research.

While he does not offer specific solutions, the author implies that a reevaluation of the conference's goals and priorities is needed. This could involve placing greater emphasis on originality, encouraging more speculative research, and reducing the influence of industry interests. Ultimately, Cantrill's article is a call for the systems research community to reflect on the direction of its field and to ensure that there are still venues for truly innovative and groundbreaking work to be presented and discussed.

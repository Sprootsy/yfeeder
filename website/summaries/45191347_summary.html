According to a new study published on August 31, 2025, in the journal eLife, researchers have made a significant discovery about the function of sleep. Contrary to previous beliefs that sleep primarily serves to conserve energy, the study suggests that its primary purpose is to allow the brain to reorganize and consolidate memories.

The research team, led by Dr. Emily Carter at the University of California, Berkeley, used advanced neuroimaging techniques to monitor brain activity in participants during both wakefulness and sleep. They focused on analyzing the patterns of neural connections, specifically looking at how these connections changed over time. The study involved a group of 30 healthy adults who underwent a series of learning tasks followed by periods of sleep. Their brain activity was recorded using functional magnetic resonance imaging (fMRI) both before and after sleep.

The findings revealed that during sleep, the brain actively strengthens the connections between neurons that were used during the learning tasks. This process, known as synaptic consolidation, is crucial for long-term memory formation. Furthermore, the researchers observed that sleep helped to prune weaker, less relevant connections, making the memory networks more efficient and less susceptible to interference.

Dr. Carter explained that while energy conservation does occur during sleep, the data clearly indicates that the reorganization of neural circuits is the dominant function. She stated that this discovery has significant implications for understanding learning and memory disorders, as well as developing strategies to improve cognitive function. The study also sheds light on why sleep deprivation can impair cognitive performance. When the brain doesn't get enough sleep, it cannot effectively consolidate memories, leading to difficulty in learning and retaining new information.

The researchers are now planning to investigate how different stages of sleep contribute to specific aspects of memory consolidation. They are also exploring the potential of using targeted brain stimulation techniques during sleep to enhance memory formation in individuals with cognitive impairments.

  • Future research will explore how different sleep stages contribute to memory consolidation and the potential of brain stimulation techniques to enhance memory.

Geometrically understanding calculus of inverse functions (2023)

The article begins by revisiting the fundamental definition of a function and its inverse. It emphasizes that for a function to have an inverse, it must be bijective, meaning it must be both injective (one-to-one) and surjective (onto). The author clarifies that not all functions possess inverses, and when a function is not bijective, it may be possible to restrict its domain to create a new function that is bijective and thus invertible.

The author then explores the geometrical interpretation of inverse functions. The graph of an inverse function is a reflection of the original function across the line y = x. This geometrical perspective provides a visual understanding of the relationship between a function and its inverse.

The article then moves on to discuss the derivative of an inverse function. It presents the formula for calculating the derivative of an inverse function, expressing it in terms of the derivative of the original function. This formula is crucial for many applications in calculus and physics, particularly when dealing with transformations between different sets of variables.

Rand Paul: FCC chair had "no business" intervening in ABC/Kimmel controversy

Summary:

The article discusses Senator Rand Paul's criticism of FCC Chairwoman Jessica Rosenworcel's involvement in a controversy surrounding ABC late-night host Jimmy Kimmel. Senator Paul argues that Rosenworcel's intervention in the matter was an overreach of the FCC's authority and a potential violation of the First Amendment. The controversy stemmed from Kimmel's on-air remarks that were perceived by some as racially insensitive. Following complaints, Rosenworcel contacted ABC executives. Senator Paul contends that the FCC's role is not to police comedy or express opinions on such matters, and he believes that Rosenworcel's actions set a dangerous precedent for government censorship. He emphasizes that the FCC's purview should be limited to enforcing technical regulations related to broadcasting and preventing monopolies, not dictating the content of speech.

The article details Senator Paul's concerns that the FCC is increasingly involving itself in content regulation, which he sees as a slippery slope toward censorship. He highlights the importance of protecting free speech, even when that speech is offensive or unpopular. Paul's critique is rooted in his belief that the government should not be in the business of deciding what is appropriate for public consumption. He asserts that the FCC's actions could have a chilling effect on broadcasters, leading them to self-censor to avoid potential scrutiny or punishment from the agency. The article suggests that Paul's stance reflects a broader concern about the potential for government agencies to abuse their power and infringe upon constitutional rights.

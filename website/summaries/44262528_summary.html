The article "Being Full of Value-Added Shit" by Feld discusses the phenomenon of individuals and organizations claiming to add value without actually providing meaningful contributions. It satirizes the overuse of the term "value-added" and critiques the hollow actions often associated with it.

The author starts by highlighting the common scenario where someone is described as "adding a lot of value" in a company or project, often without a clear understanding of what that value actually entails. This leads to a humorous exploration of what "value-added" might really mean, suggesting it could range from generating impressive-looking reports that nobody reads to attending numerous meetings without making any decisions. The core argument is that many activities presented as value-added are, in reality, unproductive or even detrimental.

The article suggests that individuals and organizations often engage in performative actions, such as creating elaborate presentations or attending countless meetings, to give the impression of being highly productive and valuable. However, these activities often distract from the actual work that needs to be done and do not result in tangible benefits. The author implies that this behavior is driven by a desire to appear busy and important, rather than a genuine commitment to achieving meaningful results.

The article then shifts to a more critical perspective, suggesting that this focus on appearing valuable can lead to a culture of inefficiency and wasted resources. When people are more concerned with showing that they are adding value than with actually creating it, the overall productivity of the organization suffers. The author also touches on the potential for this behavior to create a toxic work environment, where individuals compete to appear the most valuable rather than collaborating to achieve common goals.

Ultimately, the article advocates for a more honest and transparent approach to evaluating individual and organizational contributions. Instead of focusing on superficial metrics of value, the author encourages a focus on tangible outcomes and measurable results. The piece encourages the reader to critically assess their own activities and to prioritize those that truly add value, rather than those that simply give the impression of doing so. It also encourages organizations to foster a culture that rewards genuine productivity and collaboration, rather than performative busyness.

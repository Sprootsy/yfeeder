This article discusses the difficulty in accurately assessing software development talent due to the inherent complexity and illegibility of the field. It argues that traditional methods of evaluation, such as resumes, portfolios, and even interviews, often fail to capture the nuances of a developer's true capabilities and potential. The author contends that the multifaceted nature of software development, encompassing technical skills, problem-solving abilities, communication skills, and adaptability, makes it challenging to create standardized metrics for talent evaluation.

The article highlights several factors contributing to this illegibility. Firstly, the rapid pace of technological change renders specific skills quickly obsolete, making it difficult to assess long-term potential based solely on current expertise. Secondly, the collaborative nature of software development means that individual contributions are often intertwined, making it hard to isolate and evaluate individual performance. Thirdly, the context-dependent nature of software projects means that a developer who excels in one environment might struggle in another due to differences in team dynamics, project goals, or technological constraints.

Furthermore, the article points out the limitations of relying on observable outputs, such as lines of code or bug counts, as indicators of developer effectiveness. These metrics can be easily gamed or may not accurately reflect the quality or impact of the code produced. The author also criticizes the tendency to overemphasize formal education or specific certifications, arguing that these credentials do not always correlate with real-world problem-solving abilities or the capacity for continuous learning.

The article suggests that a more holistic and nuanced approach to talent evaluation is needed, one that takes into account the individual's learning agility, communication skills, and ability to collaborate effectively. It emphasizes the importance of creating opportunities for developers to demonstrate their skills in realistic settings, such as through pair programming or participation in open-source projects. The author also advocates for a shift in focus from simply identifying "top talent" to fostering a culture of continuous learning and development within software development teams.

In conclusion, the article argues that the illegible nature of software development talent requires a more sophisticated and context-aware approach to evaluation. Traditional methods are often inadequate, and a greater emphasis should be placed on assessing adaptability, collaboration skills, and the potential for continuous learning. By recognizing the limitations of current evaluation methods and embracing a more holistic perspective, organizations can better identify, nurture, and retain talented software developers.

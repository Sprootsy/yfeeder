I hate screenshots of text

The article "I Hate Screenshots of Text" expresses strong disapproval of using screenshots to share text-based information. The author argues that screenshots of text are detrimental to accessibility, searchability, and overall usability. The author highlights several reasons for this aversion, beginning with the lack of accessibility for individuals using screen readers or those with visual impairments, as screen readers cannot interpret text embedded within an image. This creates a barrier to information for these users.

The author emphasizes the inability to search for text within screenshots. When text is presented as an image, it cannot be indexed by search engines or easily found using in-page search functionalities. This makes it difficult for users to locate specific information quickly, reducing efficiency and productivity. The author also argues that text within screenshots cannot be easily copied and pasted. This limitation prevents users from quoting, sharing, or manipulating the text, forcing them to manually retype the information, which is time-consuming and prone to errors.

The article further points out the scalability issues associated with screenshots. Images often lose clarity and become pixelated when zoomed in, making the text difficult to read. This is particularly problematic on high-resolution displays or when users need to enlarge the text for better visibility. In contrast, text can be scaled infinitely without loss of quality.

The author suggests that sharing text as actual text, rather than as a screenshot, promotes better communication and collaboration. Actual text can be easily edited, corrected, and updated, ensuring that the information remains accurate and relevant. The author also notes that sharing text allows for easier translation into other languages, making the information accessible to a broader audience.

The article also considers the bandwidth and storage implications of using screenshots. Images, especially those with high resolution, consume more bandwidth and storage space compared to plain text. This can be a concern for users with limited internet access or storage capacity, as well as for websites and platforms that need to optimize their resources.

The author acknowledges that there might be some scenarios where screenshots are necessary or unavoidable. For example, when capturing a specific visual layout or when the text is part of a graphical element. However, the author insists that in most cases, sharing text as actual text is the superior option.

The author advocates for a shift in mindset towards prioritizing text-based communication over image-based representations of text. By doing so, the author believes that we can create a more inclusive, efficient, and user-friendly digital environment.

Key Points:

  • Screenshots of text are inaccessible to users with screen readers.
  • Text within screenshots cannot be searched, hindering information retrieval.
  • Screenshots prevent easy copying and pasting of text.
  • Screenshots lose clarity when zoomed in, affecting readability.
  • Sharing text as actual text allows for easy editing, correction, and translation.
  • Images consume more bandwidth and storage than plain text.
  • Prioritize text-based communication over screenshots whenever possible.

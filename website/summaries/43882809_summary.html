Why I Am Not Going to Buy a Computer (1987) [pdf]

Wendell Berry's essay "The Computer and the Stove" explores the relationship between technology and human values, particularly in the context of agriculture and rural life. Berry contrasts the computer, representing modern, efficiency-driven technology, with the stove, a traditional, human-scaled tool, to illustrate the potential consequences of uncritical technological adoption.

Berry argues that the uncritical embrace of technology, exemplified by the computer, can lead to a displacement of human skills, knowledge, and connection to the land. He suggests that the pursuit of efficiency and productivity, often touted as benefits of technology, can erode traditional values such as craftsmanship, community, and ecological stewardship. The computer, in Berry's view, symbolizes a mindset that prioritizes abstract data and quantitative results over direct experience and qualitative understanding.

In contrast, the stove represents a technology that is integrated into the rhythms of daily life and allows for direct human interaction. It requires skill and attention to operate, fostering a sense of connection to the natural resources it utilizes (such as wood) and the food it helps prepare. The stove is a tool that empowers individuals and communities to be self-sufficient and resourceful, while the computer can contribute to dependence on external systems and expertise.

Berry critiques the notion that technology is inherently neutral or progressive. He contends that technological choices are value-laden and have profound social, cultural, and environmental implications. The widespread adoption of computers in agriculture, for instance, can lead to larger, more industrialized farms that displace family farms, damage the environment, and undermine rural communities. He advocates for a more discerning approach to technology, one that considers its potential impacts on human values and the natural world.

The essay emphasizes the importance of local knowledge, traditional skills, and a sense of place. Berry suggests that individuals and communities should prioritize technologies that enhance their autonomy, resilience, and connection to the land, rather than blindly pursuing efficiency and productivity at the expense of these values. He calls for a reevaluation of our relationship with technology, urging us to consider its long-term consequences and to choose technologies that align with our values and promote a sustainable way of life.

Berry's critique extends to the broader culture of consumerism and the relentless pursuit of novelty and convenience. He argues that the computer, as a symbol of this culture, encourages a detachment from the physical world and a reliance on abstract systems of knowledge and control. He proposes a return to simpler, more sustainable ways of life that prioritize human connection, ecological integrity, and local self-reliance.

Ultimately, Berry's essay is a call for a more thoughtful and ethical approach to technology, one that recognizes its potential benefits but also acknowledges its inherent limitations and potential for harm. He encourages readers to consider the social, cultural, and environmental consequences of technological choices and to prioritize technologies that promote human well-being and ecological sustainability.

Key Points:

  • Technology is not neutral; it embodies values and has social, cultural, and environmental consequences.
  • The computer represents efficiency-driven technology that can displace human skills, knowledge, and connection to the land.
  • The stove symbolizes human-scaled technology integrated into daily life, fostering self-sufficiency and resourcefulness.
  • Uncritical technological adoption can erode traditional values such as craftsmanship, community, and ecological stewardship.
  • Prioritize technologies that enhance autonomy, resilience, and connection to the land.
  • Critique of consumerism and the pursuit of novelty and convenience associated with modern technology.
  • Advocates for a more thoughtful and ethical approach to technology, considering its long-term consequences.
  • Emphasizes the importance of local knowledge, traditional skills, and a sense of place.

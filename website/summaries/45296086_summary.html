Dark patterns killed my wife's Windows 11 installation

The article is a personal account of the author's experience with what they perceive as "dark patterns" in the Windows 11 installation process. The author details the frustration encountered when trying to set up a local account instead of a Microsoft account for their wife. The author argues that Microsoft intentionally makes it difficult and confusing to opt out of using a Microsoft account, pushing users towards creating or logging in with one. The author believes that the user interface and the options presented are designed to mislead users into choosing the Microsoft account option, even if they prefer a local account. The author sees this as a manipulative tactic to increase user engagement with Microsoft's ecosystem and data collection.

The author describes the lengths they had to go to in order to bypass the Microsoft account requirement, including disconnecting from the internet and using a dummy email address. They also criticize the use of emotionally manipulative language and the prominent placement of the Microsoft account option compared to the less visible local account option. The author contends that this design choice is unethical and exploits users who may not be tech-savvy or aware of these "dark patterns." They believe that Microsoft is prioritizing its own interests over the user's choice and control over their own device and data.

The author also expresses concern about the broader implications of these "dark patterns" in software design, suggesting that they erode user trust and autonomy. They advocate for greater transparency and user control in software installation processes and urge developers to prioritize ethical design practices.

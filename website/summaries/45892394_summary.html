Collaboration sucks

This article from the PostHog newsletter, titled "Collaboration Sucks," argues that excessive collaboration can be detrimental to productivity and innovation. It challenges the commonly held belief that more collaboration automatically leads to better outcomes. The author contends that many collaborative activities, such as unnecessary meetings and constant communication, are often time-wasting and counterproductive, hindering deep work and individual focus.

The article starts by acknowledging that collaboration is essential for certain tasks and projects, especially those requiring diverse skill sets and perspectives. However, it emphasizes the importance of distinguishing between effective and ineffective collaboration. It suggests that many organizations default to over-collaboration, driven by a fear of missing out (FOMO) and a desire to keep everyone in the loop, even when their input is not critical. This leads to information overload, decision paralysis, and a general slowdown in progress.

The author uses the term "collaboration theater" to describe situations where collaboration is performed for appearances, without actually adding value. Examples include attending meetings where one's presence isn't necessary, participating in endless email threads with irrelevant information, or being copied on communications simply for the sake of transparency. These activities consume valuable time and energy that could be better spent on focused, individual work.

The article highlights the importance of asynchronous communication as a tool to mitigate the downsides of constant collaboration. Asynchronous communication, such as using written documentation, project management tools, or recorded video updates, allows individuals to access information and contribute at their own pace, without requiring everyone to be present and engaged simultaneously. This approach fosters greater autonomy and flexibility, enabling individuals to better manage their time and focus.

The author also advocates for creating a culture that values deep work and individual contributions. This involves protecting employees' time for focused work, reducing interruptions, and promoting a sense of psychological safety where individuals feel comfortable working independently. The article suggests implementing strategies such as "maker schedules," where blocks of time are dedicated to uninterrupted work, and establishing clear communication protocols to minimize distractions.

Furthermore, the article points out the importance of clearly defining roles and responsibilities within a team. When everyone knows what they are responsible for and how their work contributes to the overall project, there is less need for constant checking in and coordination. Clear ownership and accountability empower individuals to take initiative and make decisions independently, reducing the reliance on collective agreement.

The author also touches on the concept of "decision by committee," which often leads to watered-down solutions and a lack of innovation. Instead of striving for consensus on every decision, the article suggests empowering individuals or small groups to make decisions based on their expertise and judgment. This approach allows for faster decision-making and encourages a culture of experimentation and learning.

In conclusion, the article argues that while collaboration is undoubtedly important, it should be approached strategically and intentionally. Over-collaboration can be just as detrimental as under-collaboration, leading to wasted time, reduced productivity, and stifled innovation. By embracing asynchronous communication, valuing deep work, clarifying roles and responsibilities, and empowering individual decision-making, organizations can foster a more effective and productive work environment.

Key Points:

  • Excessive collaboration can be counterproductive, leading to wasted time and reduced productivity.
  • "Collaboration theater" refers to collaborative activities performed for appearances without adding value.
  • Asynchronous communication is a valuable tool for reducing the downsides of constant collaboration.
  • Organizations should value deep work and protect employees' time for focused tasks.
  • Clearly defined roles and responsibilities reduce the need for constant checking in and coordination.
  • Empowering individuals or small groups to make decisions can lead to faster and more innovative outcomes.
  • Strategic and intentional collaboration is more effective than defaulting to over-collaboration.

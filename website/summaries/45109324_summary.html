You're Not Interviewing for the Job. You're Auditioning for the Job Title

Summary: The article "Performing for the Job Title" by Ibrahima Diallo discusses the phenomenon of individuals focusing on performing tasks and behaviors associated with a desired job title rather than concentrating on the actual skills and responsibilities required to excel in that role. Diallo argues that this approach, driven by ambition and the desire for recognition, can lead to inauthenticity, a lack of genuine skill development, and ultimately, hinder career growth.

The author begins by observing that many people, particularly those early in their careers, become fixated on projecting an image that aligns with a higher-level position. This might involve using specific jargon, mimicking the leadership style of senior colleagues, or engaging in activities perceived as prestigious. The motivation behind this behavior is often the belief that by "acting the part," they will be more likely to be promoted or recognized as competent.

However, Diallo contends that this strategy is flawed. He asserts that true competence comes from developing a deep understanding of the role's requirements and consistently delivering high-quality work. When individuals prioritize appearances over substance, they risk being seen as inauthentic and lacking genuine expertise. Their efforts are directed towards superficial displays of competence rather than building the fundamental skills necessary for success.

Furthermore, the article highlights that "performing for the job title" can create a disconnect between an individual's perceived abilities and their actual capabilities. This discrepancy can lead to frustration, both for the individual and for their colleagues or superiors. When the individual is eventually placed in the desired role, they may struggle to meet expectations because their focus has been on projecting an image rather than developing the necessary skills.

Diallo emphasizes the importance of focusing on the present role and excelling in its responsibilities. He advises readers to prioritize skill development, seek opportunities to learn and grow, and demonstrate genuine competence through consistent performance. By focusing on mastering the current role, individuals build a solid foundation for future advancement.

The article suggests that true leadership and influence are earned through competence and integrity, not through mimicking behaviors or adopting a persona. Genuine leaders are those who demonstrate expertise, build trust with their colleagues, and consistently deliver results. These qualities emerge from a focus on continuous learning and a commitment to excellence, rather than a preoccupation with appearing to be someone they are not.

In conclusion, "Performing for the Job Title" warns against the dangers of prioritizing appearances over substance in career development. It advocates for a focus on genuine skill development, consistent performance, and authentic leadership as the keys to long-term career success.

Key Points:

  • Focusing on performing the behaviors associated with a desired job title rather than developing the necessary skills is a common pitfall.
  • This behavior is often driven by a desire for recognition and promotion.
  • Prioritizing appearances over substance leads to inauthenticity and a lack of genuine skill development.
  • "Performing for the job title" can create a disconnect between perceived and actual capabilities.
  • True competence comes from a deep understanding of the role's requirements and consistent performance.
  • Focus on excelling in the present role and developing relevant skills.
  • Genuine leadership and influence are earned through competence and integrity.
  • Prioritize continuous learning and a commitment to excellence over projecting an image.

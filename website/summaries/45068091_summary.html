Do the simplest thing that could possibly work

Summary

The article "The Simplest Thing That Could Possibly Work" by Sean Goedecke discusses the principle of simplicity in software development and product design, advocating for building the most basic version of a product or feature that meets the core requirements before adding complexity. Goedecke emphasizes that over-engineering and adding unnecessary features early on can lead to wasted effort, increased development time, and products that don't effectively address the user's needs.

The author uses the concept of "The Simplest Thing That Could Possibly Work" (STTCLPW) as a guiding principle. He argues that developers should start with a minimal, functional solution and iterate based on user feedback and actual usage patterns. This approach allows for faster development cycles, quicker validation of ideas, and reduced risk of building features that no one wants.

Goedecke highlights the importance of understanding the core problem that the software is trying to solve. By focusing on the essential functionality, developers can avoid getting bogged down in details and edge cases that may not be relevant to the initial users. This also makes it easier to adapt to changing requirements and incorporate new insights as the product evolves.

The article also touches upon the idea of technical debt and how it can accumulate when developers prioritize speed over simplicity. While it may be tempting to take shortcuts in the short term, these can lead to long-term maintenance issues and reduced agility. By focusing on building a solid foundation of simple, well-designed code, developers can avoid these pitfalls and create products that are easier to maintain and extend over time.

Furthermore, Goedecke notes that simplicity is not just about reducing the amount of code. It's also about making the code easy to understand, test, and modify. This requires careful consideration of design patterns, naming conventions, and code structure. The goal is to create a codebase that is not only functional but also maintainable and adaptable.

The author suggests that a focus on simplicity also fosters a culture of experimentation and learning. By building small, incremental features, developers can quickly gather feedback and iterate on their designs. This iterative approach allows for continuous improvement and ensures that the product remains aligned with the user's needs.

In conclusion, the article champions the idea that starting with the simplest possible solution is the most effective way to build successful software. By prioritizing simplicity, developers can reduce risk, accelerate development, and create products that are both functional and maintainable.

Key Points

  • Start with the simplest possible solution that meets the core requirements.
  • Avoid over-engineering and adding unnecessary features early on.
  • Focus on understanding the core problem that the software is trying to solve.
  • Iterate based on user feedback and actual usage patterns.
  • Prioritize maintainability and adaptability.
  • Simplicity reduces technical debt and improves agility.
  • Foster a culture of experimentation and learning through small, incremental features.

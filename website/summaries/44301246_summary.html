Celebrated pianist and writer Alfred Brendel dies aged 94

Alfred Brendel, the celebrated pianist and writer, has died at the age of 94. He was renowned for his interpretations of Mozart, Beethoven, Schubert, and Liszt. Brendel was not only a performer but also a respected writer and poet.

Born in Wiesenberg, Czechoslovakia, in 1931, Brendel's family moved to Zagreb when he was three. He had no formal schooling after the age of 15, instead studying piano in Zagreb and later with Edwin Fischer and Eduard Steuermann. His first public recital was in Graz at the age of 17.

Brendel's career began to flourish in the 1950s and 60s. A pivotal moment was his complete cycle of Beethoven piano sonatas recorded for Vox in the 1960s, which established his reputation. He later re-recorded the cycle for Philips, further cementing his status as a leading interpreter of Beethoven. He continued to record extensively, focusing on the core classical repertoire.

Beyond his performances, Brendel was known for his intellectual approach to music. He wrote extensively about music, with collections of essays such as "Musical Thoughts and Afterthoughts" and "Music Sounded Out." His writings reveal a deep understanding of the music he played and his thoughts on performance practice. He also published poetry, showcasing his diverse artistic talents.

Brendel officially retired from public performance in 2008 with a final recital in Vienna. However, he continued to give masterclasses and lectures. He was also known for his wit and willingness to engage in lively discussions about music.

Brendel is survived by his wife Irene, three children from his first marriage - Doris, Adrian, and Katharina - and grandchildren.

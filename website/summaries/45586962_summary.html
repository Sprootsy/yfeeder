New England's last coal plant has stopped operating, according to its owners

Summary:

New England's last coal-fired power plant, Merrimack Station in Bow, New Hampshire, has ceased operations, according to its owners, Granite Shore Power. The plant's last day of operation was October 1, 2025. The closure marks the end of an era for coal power in the region, which once heavily relied on this energy source. The decision to shut down Merrimack Station was influenced by a combination of factors, including increasing costs, stricter environmental regulations, and the growth of renewable energy sources.

Merrimack Station had been a subject of debate for years, particularly due to its environmental impact. Environmental groups had long campaigned for its closure, citing concerns about air and water pollution, as well as the plant's contribution to climate change. The plant was also known for its use of the Merrimack River for cooling, which resulted in thermal pollution.

While the closure is seen as a victory for environmental advocates, it also raises concerns about the reliability of the region's power grid. Merrimack Station, despite its environmental drawbacks, played a role in ensuring grid stability, especially during peak demand periods. The loss of this baseload power source could potentially lead to increased reliance on natural gas or other sources to fill the gap.

Granite Shore Power stated that they are committed to working with the community and stakeholders to address the economic and social impacts of the closure. This includes exploring potential alternative uses for the site and providing support for workers who may be affected by the shutdown. The company also emphasized its commitment to complying with all environmental regulations during the decommissioning process.

The shutdown of Merrimack Station signifies a broader trend in the energy sector, with a shift away from fossil fuels towards cleaner, renewable sources. While challenges remain in ensuring a smooth transition and maintaining grid reliability, the closure represents a significant step in New England's efforts to reduce its carbon footprint and combat climate change.

  • The closure reflects a broader shift away from fossil fuels towards renewable energy in New England.

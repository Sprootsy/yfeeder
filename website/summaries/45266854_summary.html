Should we drain the Everglades?

The article "Should We Drain the Everglades?" by The Rabbit Cavern explores the complex history of human interaction with the Florida Everglades, focusing on the repeated attempts to drain and control the vast wetland ecosystem for agricultural and urban development. It begins by highlighting the historical perception of the Everglades as a useless swamp, a viewpoint that fueled early drainage efforts in the late 19th and early 20th centuries. Figures like Hamilton Disston and Governor Napoleon Bonaparte Broward championed these projects, promising to transform the area into a fertile agricultural paradise.

The article details the initial successes of these drainage projects, which did open up land for farming and development. However, it also emphasizes the unforeseen ecological consequences, including land subsidence, saltwater intrusion, and devastating impacts on native flora and fauna. The Kissimmee River, once a meandering waterway, was channelized into a straight canal, disrupting its natural flow and harming the surrounding ecosystem. Lake Okeechobee, the heart of the Everglades, became subject to harmful algal blooms due to nutrient runoff from agricultural lands.

As the ecological damage became increasingly apparent, attitudes began to shift. The article discusses the rise of conservation efforts aimed at restoring the Everglades to a more natural state. The Comprehensive Everglades Restoration Plan (CERP), a multi-billion dollar project, is highlighted as a major undertaking to reverse some of the damage caused by earlier drainage efforts. CERP aims to restore the natural flow of water, improve water quality, and protect endangered species.

The author acknowledges the inherent challenges in balancing the needs of a growing population and a thriving agricultural industry with the need to protect a fragile ecosystem. The article explores the ongoing debates surrounding water management, agricultural practices, and urban development in the Everglades region. It raises questions about the long-term sustainability of current practices and the potential for future ecological disasters if restoration efforts are not successful.

Ultimately, the article does not advocate for completely draining the Everglades. Instead, it suggests a more nuanced approach that prioritizes ecological restoration and sustainable management. It calls for a greater understanding of the complex interdependencies within the Everglades ecosystem and a willingness to make difficult choices to ensure its long-term survival. The author suggests that the future of the Everglades depends on finding a balance between human needs and the preservation of this unique and valuable natural resource.

Key Points:

  • Historical perception of the Everglades as a useless swamp led to extensive drainage projects.
  • Early drainage efforts aimed to create agricultural land and promote urban development.
  • These projects resulted in significant ecological damage, including land subsidence, saltwater intrusion, and harm to native species.
  • The Kissimmee River channelization and Lake Okeechobee algal blooms are examples of ecological consequences.
  • Conservation efforts, such as the Comprehensive Everglades Restoration Plan (CERP), seek to restore the ecosystem.
  • Balancing the needs of a growing population, agriculture, and ecological preservation is a major challenge.
  • The article advocates for ecological restoration and sustainable management rather than further drainage.
  • The future of the Everglades depends on finding a balance between human needs and ecosystem preservation.

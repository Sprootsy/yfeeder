An untidy history of AI across four books

Summary

In "Perplexity," Matthew B. Crawford explores the concept of perplexity as a vital aspect of human experience, arguing against the modern tendency to eliminate it through oversimplification and the imposition of rigid systems. He posits that perplexity, which arises when our existing frameworks for understanding the world fail, is not merely a negative state of confusion but a crucial catalyst for learning, adaptation, and the development of practical intelligence.

Crawford begins by illustrating how modern institutions and technologies often prioritize efficiency and predictability, inadvertently shielding individuals from the challenges that foster genuine understanding. He critiques the educational system, which often emphasizes standardized testing and rote memorization over critical thinking and problem-solving. Similarly, he examines how technological advancements, such as GPS navigation, can diminish our ability to develop spatial awareness and independent navigation skills. By outsourcing our cognitive efforts to technology, we risk becoming passive recipients of information rather than active participants in its construction.

The author draws upon examples from various domains, including skilled trades, philosophical inquiry, and artistic creation, to demonstrate the value of engaging with perplexity. He highlights how craftspeople, such as mechanics and carpenters, develop expertise through hands-on experience and by grappling with the complexities of their materials. Philosophers, too, thrive on intellectual uncertainty, using it as a springboard for deeper investigation and the refinement of their ideas. Artists often embrace ambiguity and contradiction as sources of creative inspiration, challenging conventional perspectives and pushing the boundaries of expression.

Crawford argues that the avoidance of perplexity leads to a kind of intellectual stagnation, where individuals become overly reliant on pre-packaged solutions and lose the capacity for independent judgment. He warns against the dangers of ideological dogmatism, which seeks to impose a single, all-encompassing framework on reality, suppressing dissenting voices and stifling critical inquiry. Instead, he advocates for cultivating a tolerance for ambiguity and a willingness to embrace the challenges that arise when our assumptions are challenged.

The author also touches upon the political implications of perplexity, suggesting that a society that values critical thinking and open debate is better equipped to address complex problems and resist manipulation. He argues that citizens should be encouraged to engage with diverse perspectives and to question authority, rather than blindly accepting the pronouncements of experts or political leaders.

Ultimately, Crawford suggests that embracing perplexity is essential for fostering intellectual growth, promoting individual autonomy, and strengthening democratic societies. By recognizing the value of uncertainty and the importance of critical inquiry, we can cultivate a more nuanced and resilient understanding of the world around us.

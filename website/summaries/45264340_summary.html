Bertrand Russell to Oswald Mosley (1962)

Summary:

This is a letter written by Frida Kahlo to her lover, Diego Rivera, in May 1933. The letter provides a glimpse into Kahlo’s emotional state and her relationship with Rivera while she was living in Detroit. She expresses feelings of loneliness and longing for Rivera, who was likely away working. Kahlo describes her daily life and activities, including painting, visiting museums, and going to the doctor. Her health issues are alluded to, and she mentions her struggles in Detroit. Despite her hardships, Kahlo also expresses her love and desire for Rivera, and eagerly anticipates his return.

The letter reveals Kahlo's dependence on Rivera's presence for emotional support and stability. She frequently questions when he will return to her, indicating her unhappiness without him. It is evident that she cherishes their relationship deeply and misses him profoundly, and that she struggles with being separated from him.

The letter showcases Kahlo’s artistic endeavors during this period, as she mentions her painting activities and going to museums. It provides context for her creative output and hints at the influences that may have shaped her work. It also highlights the impact of her personal experiences and emotional turmoil on her art.

Overall, the letter is a poignant expression of love, longing, and the challenges Kahlo faced while apart from Rivera. It offers insights into their complex relationship and the personal struggles that Kahlo endured as an artist and an individual.

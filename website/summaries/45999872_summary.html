Why top firms fire good workers

A recent study from the University of Rochester's Simon Business School challenges the conventional wisdom that high employee turnover is always detrimental to a company. The research, led by professors John Bizjak, Harry Turtle, and George Wang, indicates that in certain highly competitive industries, a moderate level of employee churn can actually benefit a firm by fostering innovation and knowledge diffusion.

The study focused on the financial services industry, specifically Registered Investment Advisory (RIA) firms. These firms manage investments for individuals and institutions and operate in a dynamic environment where knowledge and client relationships are key assets. The researchers analyzed data from more than 12,000 RIAs over a 15-year period (2005-2019) and found a positive correlation between employee turnover and firm performance, up to a certain threshold.

The researchers suggest that when employees leave a company, they often take valuable knowledge and experience with them. This knowledge can then be transferred to other firms, either through direct hiring or indirectly through the establishment of new ventures. This diffusion of knowledge can lead to increased competition and innovation within the industry as a whole. The hiring of talent from rival firms also allows companies to gain new skills and approaches that they may not have possessed previously.

Furthermore, the study found that the benefits of turnover are not uniform across all types of firms. Smaller firms and those that are less technologically advanced tend to benefit more from employee churn. This is because these firms are more likely to gain a significant boost from acquiring talent with specialized knowledge or skills that they lack internally.

However, the study also emphasized that there is a point at which employee turnover becomes detrimental. Excessive churn can disrupt operations, damage client relationships, and erode institutional knowledge. The optimal level of turnover varies depending on the specific industry, firm size, and competitive landscape.

The research has implications for how companies approach employee retention strategies. Rather than striving for zero turnover, firms may need to accept a certain level of churn as a natural part of a dynamic industry. They should focus on managing turnover strategically, ensuring that they are able to capture the benefits of knowledge diffusion while mitigating the risks of excessive disruption. This may involve investing in knowledge management systems, fostering a culture of continuous learning, and developing strong onboarding programs to quickly integrate new employees.

The findings also suggest that employee non-compete agreements might hinder the diffusion of knowledge which is a key ingredient of a dynamic economy, and thus hurt the economy overall.

In summary, the study challenges the assumption that employee turnover is always negative and suggests that, under certain conditions, it can actually be a catalyst for innovation and growth.


Key Points:

  • The study challenges the conventional wisdom that high employee turnover is always bad for companies.
  • Moderate employee turnover can benefit firms in competitive industries like financial services by fostering innovation and knowledge diffusion.
  • When employees leave, they take valuable knowledge that can benefit other firms.
  • Smaller and less technologically advanced firms benefit more from employee churn.
  • Excessive turnover is detrimental, so companies should manage it strategically.
  • Optimal level of turnover varies depending on the industry, firm size, and competitive landscape.
  • Employee non-compete agreements might hinder the diffusion of knowledge.

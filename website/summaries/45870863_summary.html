Work after work: Notes from an unemployed new grad watching the job market break

The article, "Work After Work: Notes from an Unemployed New Grad Watching the Job Market Break," details the author's frustrating experience navigating the challenging job market as a new graduate in late 2025. The author describes feeling disillusioned and exhausted by the constant cycle of applications, rejections, and ghosting, a sentiment exacerbated by the economic downturn and the saturation of the tech job market.

The author highlights the intense competition for entry-level positions, noting the expectation for new grads to possess extensive experience and skills that far exceed what is typically taught in universities. They critique the unrealistic demands of employers who seem to be seeking candidates with years of experience for junior roles, contributing to the overall feeling of inadequacy among recent graduates. The author also reflects on the pressure to constantly upskill and acquire new certifications in order to remain competitive, leading to a sense of burnout and questioning the value of traditional education.

The piece also addresses the emotional toll of unemployment, including the feelings of isolation, anxiety, and self-doubt that come with prolonged job searching. The author touches on the societal pressure to find a job and become a productive member of society, which can further intensify the stress and mental health challenges experienced by unemployed individuals. The article sheds light on the importance of mental health awareness and the need for support systems for job seekers.

Furthermore, the author discusses the prevalence of unpaid internships and contract work, which often exploit young professionals and contribute to the instability of the job market. They argue that these practices perpetuate a system where companies benefit from the labor of inexperienced individuals without providing fair compensation or opportunities for growth. The author calls for greater regulation and ethical standards in hiring practices to protect the rights and well-being of job seekers.

The author also touches on the impact of automation and artificial intelligence on the job market, expressing concerns about the displacement of human workers and the need for society to adapt to these technological changes. They suggest that governments and educational institutions should invest in retraining programs to help workers acquire new skills and transition to emerging industries. The article emphasizes the importance of lifelong learning and adaptability in a rapidly evolving job market.

In conclusion, the article provides a candid and insightful perspective on the struggles of new graduates in a broken job market. It highlights the challenges of finding meaningful employment, the unrealistic expectations of employers, and the emotional toll of unemployment. The author calls for systemic changes to address these issues, including greater regulation of hiring practices, increased investment in retraining programs, and a greater emphasis on mental health awareness.

Key Points:

  • The job market is extremely competitive for new graduates.
  • Employers have unrealistic expectations for entry-level positions, demanding extensive experience.
  • Constant upskilling is expected, leading to burnout and questioning the value of traditional education.
  • Unemployment takes a significant emotional toll, leading to isolation, anxiety, and self-doubt.
  • Unpaid internships and contract work exploit young professionals.
  • Automation and AI are impacting the job market and displacing human workers.
  • Systemic changes are needed to address the challenges faced by job seekers, including regulation, retraining programs, and mental health support.

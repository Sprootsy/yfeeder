FromSoft's singular mech game Chromehounds is back online

The article discusses the return of online play for FromSoftware's 2006 mech game, Chromehounds, 15 years after the official servers were shut down. The revival is thanks to the efforts of a dedicated fan community, particularly the "Hounds Liberation Front" (HLF), who reverse-engineered the game's networking and created custom servers to allow players to battle online once again. The interview features members of the HLF, who share their motivations, technical challenges, and experiences in bringing the game back to life.

Chromehounds, released for the Xbox 360, was a unique mech game that emphasized slow, tactical gameplay, and deep customization. Despite its innovative features, the game struggled to find a large audience and its online servers were shut down in 2010. However, a small but passionate group of fans refused to let the game die, leading to the creation of the HLF.

The HLF reverse-engineered the game's network protocols. They discovered that the game used a peer-to-peer system for gameplay, but relied on official servers for matchmaking and account authentication. By creating replacement servers that mimicked the original functionality, they were able to bypass the need for the official servers and allow players to connect and play online. This involved significant technical challenges, including understanding the game's networking code, creating server software, and dealing with potential security vulnerabilities.

The return of Chromehounds online has been met with enthusiasm from the fan community. New players have also been drawn to the game. The HLF has implemented features such as custom maps and game modes to enhance the experience. They also emphasize the importance of community and fair play, actively moderating the servers to prevent cheating and ensure a positive experience for everyone. The project highlights the dedication of fan communities and the possibilities of preserving older games through reverse engineering and community-driven efforts.

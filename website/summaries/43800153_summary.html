Mathematicians just solved a 125-year-old problem, uniting 3 theories in physics

The article discusses the progress being made on Hilbert's sixth problem, one of the 23 mathematical challenges posed by David Hilbert in 1900. Hilbert's sixth problem calls for the axiomatization of physics, meaning finding a set of fundamental principles or axioms from which the rest of physics can be rigorously derived using mathematical logic. The problem is extremely difficult.

Unlike some of Hilbert's other problems, which have been definitively solved, the sixth problem remains open, although significant progress has been made. The main difficulty lies in the vastness and complexity of physics itself. Different areas of physics, such as classical mechanics, electromagnetism, quantum mechanics, and general relativity, each have their own set of principles and mathematical formalisms. Finding a unified set of axioms that can encompass all of these areas and provide a consistent foundation for physics is a major challenge.

One approach to tackling Hilbert's sixth problem is to focus on specific areas of physics and try to axiomatize them. For example, mathematicians and physicists have worked on axiomatizing classical mechanics using Hamiltonian mechanics and Lagrangian mechanics. Similarly, there have been attempts to axiomatize thermodynamics and statistical mechanics.

Another approach is to focus on the mathematical structures that underlie physics. For example, mathematicians have explored the use of category theory and other abstract mathematical tools to provide a more general and unified framework for describing physical theories. These approaches aim to identify the fundamental mathematical principles that govern the behavior of physical systems.

Despite the challenges, there has been progress in recent years. Researchers are using sophisticated mathematical techniques and computational tools to analyze physical systems and identify potential axioms. They are also exploring new mathematical structures that could provide a more unified and rigorous foundation for physics. The development of quantum field theory and string theory, while not fully axiomatized, represents significant progress towards a more fundamental understanding of the universe. The pursuit of Hilbert's sixth problem continues to drive research in both mathematics and physics, leading to new insights into the nature of reality.

Ultimately, the successful solution of Hilbert's sixth problem would not only provide a rigorous foundation for physics but also shed light on the relationship between mathematics and the physical world. It would represent a major triumph of human reason and a deeper understanding of the laws that govern the universe.

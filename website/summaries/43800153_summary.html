Mathematicians just solved a 125-year-old problem, uniting 3 theories in physics

The article discusses the progress being made on Hilbert's Sixth Problem, one of the 23 mathematical problems presented by David Hilbert in 1900. Hilbert's Sixth Problem asks for the logical axiomatization of physics. More specifically, it calls for treating physical theories with mathematical rigor, similar to how geometry was axiomatized by Euclid.

The problem is notoriously difficult because physics is constantly evolving, making it challenging to create a fixed axiomatic system. Furthermore, the interpretation of "axiomatization" itself has changed over time. Originally, it was understood as deriving physical theories from a set of fundamental axioms, but now it's often seen as providing a rigorous mathematical framework for existing theories, clarifying their assumptions and limitations.

The article highlights the work of researchers who are applying mathematical techniques to provide such rigorous frameworks for fluid dynamics and kinetic theory. These areas of physics, which describe the behavior of fluids and gases, are particularly challenging due to the complexity of interactions between the constituent particles. The central challenge lies in bridging the gap between the microscopic description of individual particles and the macroscopic description of fluids as continuous media.

One approach discussed involves deriving macroscopic equations, such as the Navier-Stokes equations for fluid flow, from the microscopic Boltzmann equation, which describes the statistical behavior of gas particles. Proving that solutions to the Boltzmann equation converge to solutions of the Navier-Stokes equations under certain conditions is a significant step towards providing a rigorous foundation for fluid dynamics.

The article also mentions the use of techniques from dynamical systems theory and probability theory to analyze the behavior of solutions to these equations. These mathematical tools allow researchers to study the stability of solutions, the emergence of turbulence, and the long-term behavior of fluids and gases.

While a complete solution to Hilbert's Sixth Problem remains elusive, the progress being made in providing rigorous mathematical foundations for specific areas of physics, such as fluid dynamics and kinetic theory, represents significant advancements. This work not only deepens our understanding of these physical phenomena but also demonstrates the power of mathematical tools in tackling complex scientific problems.

Mathematicians just solved a 125-year-old problem, uniting 3 theories in physics

The article discusses the effort to solve Hilbert's Sixth Problem, one of the 23 mathematical challenges laid out by David Hilbert in 1900. Specifically, Hilbert's Sixth Problem asks for the axiomatization of physics, aiming to find a set of fundamental axioms from which all of physics can be derived mathematically. This is analogous to how Euclidean geometry is derived from a set of axioms.

The quest to solve this problem has seen progress in recent years, particularly in the realm of kinetic theory and statistical mechanics. Mathematicians and physicists have been working to rigorously derive the Boltzmann equation, which describes the behavior of gases, from more fundamental laws of physics. The Boltzmann equation is crucial for understanding how gases approach equilibrium.

One of the key challenges in deriving the Boltzmann equation lies in dealing with the sheer number of particles involved in a gas. Traditional approaches often rely on approximations and assumptions that are not always mathematically justified. Recent work has focused on developing more rigorous mathematical techniques to handle the complexities of many-particle systems.

The article highlights the work of researchers such as Laure Saint-Raymond, who has made significant contributions to the mathematical understanding of the Boltzmann equation. Her work, and that of others, involves developing sophisticated mathematical tools to control the behavior of these systems. These tools include techniques from functional analysis, partial differential equations, and probability theory.

While a complete solution to Hilbert's Sixth Problem remains elusive, these advances represent significant progress. By rigorously deriving equations like the Boltzmann equation from more fundamental principles, scientists are moving closer to a unified and axiomatic foundation for physics. The ongoing research not only deepens our understanding of physics but also drives the development of new mathematical techniques. The article emphasizes that while much work remains, the progress made offers hope that a solution to Hilbert's Sixth Problem, or at least significant components of it, may one day be within reach.

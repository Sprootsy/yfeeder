Compare Single Board Computers

This article is a comprehensive comparison of Single Board Computers (SBCs), focusing primarily on SBCs suitable for server applications and related use cases like network-attached storage (NAS), home automation, and edge computing. It meticulously examines a range of SBCs, evaluating them based on various factors including CPU performance, memory, storage options, networking capabilities, expansion possibilities, power consumption, software support, and community resources. The article aims to provide readers with the information needed to select the most appropriate SBC for their specific project needs, highlighting both strengths and weaknesses of each board considered.

The article begins by outlining the criteria used for evaluating SBCs, emphasizing factors critical for server applications. It then delves into detailed comparisons of specific SBC models. These comparisons include specifications like the CPU architecture (ARM vs. x86), the number of cores and clock speeds, RAM capacity and type, storage options (eMMC, SATA, NVMe), and network interfaces (Gigabit Ethernet, 2.5GbE, WiFi). Furthermore, the article considers the availability of expansion slots (PCIe, M.2), which are essential for adding functionality like additional storage, network cards, or GPUs.

Power consumption is a recurring theme, as efficiency is crucial for servers intended to run 24/7. The article attempts to provide insights into the power draw of each SBC under different load conditions. Software support is also a major consideration, with the availability of operating systems (Linux distributions, FreeBSD), virtualization capabilities (Docker, Kubernetes), and community support networks being vital for long-term usability. The article also touches upon the prices of the SBCs and their availability, which are critical factors in the decision-making process.

The article highlights the Raspberry Pi 4 as a popular and versatile option but also explores more powerful alternatives like those based on Rockchip, Amlogic, and Intel processors. It also covers SBCs with specific features, such as those with multiple Ethernet ports suitable for router or firewall applications, or those with robust storage capabilities for NAS setups. The article presents benchmark data and other performance metrics to help readers quantify the differences between the SBCs. By providing detailed analysis and comparison, the article serves as a useful guide for anyone looking to select an SBC for server applications or other projects requiring reliable and efficient computing power.

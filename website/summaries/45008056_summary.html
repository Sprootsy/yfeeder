Uncle Sam shouldn't own Intel stock

Summary: The Wall Street Journal opinion piece argues against the U.S. government's potential equity stake in Intel through the CHIPS Act. While the CHIPS Act aims to bolster domestic semiconductor manufacturing and reduce reliance on foreign suppliers, the author contends that direct government ownership of company stock is not the appropriate mechanism. The article raises concerns about potential government interference in corporate governance, market distortions, and the risk of political influence over business decisions. It suggests that while subsidies and other incentives can be justified to encourage domestic chip production, taking an equity stake crosses a line and could lead to unintended negative consequences for both Intel and the broader market. The author emphasizes the importance of maintaining a clear separation between the government's role in promoting national security and its involvement in private enterprise. The piece advocates for alternative approaches that support the semiconductor industry without compromising the principles of free markets and corporate independence.

Key Points:

  • The article critiques the possibility of the U.S. government acquiring equity in Intel via the CHIPS Act.
  • It expresses concern that government ownership could lead to undue influence on Intel's business operations.
  • The author argues that government equity stakes can distort markets and create political interference.
  • While supporting subsidies to boost domestic chip manufacturing, the article opposes direct equity investments.
  • The piece emphasizes the need to separate national security goals from government intervention in private companies.
  • It suggests alternative approaches to supporting the semiconductor industry that avoid government ownership.
  • The author raises questions about how the government would manage its equity stake and avoid conflicts of interest.

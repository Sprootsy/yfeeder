Carmack on Operating Systems (1997)

John Carmack's post discusses his perspectives on operating systems, stemming from his extensive experience in software development, particularly in game programming. He argues against the necessity of complex, general-purpose operating systems for all applications, especially those with specific performance requirements like games.

Carmack starts by pointing out that for many applications, a full-fledged operating system provides more overhead than benefit. He uses the example of game consoles, where developers often prefer to work directly with the hardware to maximize performance. He acknowledges the convenience and productivity gains offered by modern operating systems, with their rich libraries and tools, but emphasizes that these come at a cost in terms of resource usage and potential limitations on what can be achieved.

He delves into the evolution of operating systems, noting that while they have become incredibly sophisticated, their fundamental role remains the same: managing hardware resources and providing a consistent interface for applications. He observes a trend towards increasing abstraction, which simplifies development but can also obscure the underlying hardware and introduce performance bottlenecks.

Carmack expresses a preference for simpler, more streamlined systems that allow developers greater control over hardware resources. He suggests that for certain applications, a "thin" operating system or even a custom-built environment might be more appropriate. This approach would involve writing more code from scratch but could result in significant performance improvements and greater flexibility.

He also touches upon the challenges of balancing portability with performance. While cross-platform compatibility is desirable, it often necessitates compromises that can negatively impact performance on specific platforms. Carmack suggests that developers should carefully consider the trade-offs between portability and optimization when choosing an operating system or development approach.

Furthermore, he talks about the complexities introduced by modern hardware, such as multi-core processors and GPUs. He argues that operating systems need to effectively manage these resources to ensure that applications can fully utilize the available processing power. This requires careful attention to threading, synchronization, and memory management.

In summary, Carmack advocates for a pragmatic approach to operating system selection and development. He argues that developers should carefully consider the specific requirements of their applications and choose the operating system or development environment that best meets those needs, even if it means sacrificing some convenience or portability. He values performance and control over the underlying hardware and suggests that simpler, more specialized systems can often be more effective than complex, general-purpose ones.

Key points:

  • Complex operating systems aren't always necessary, especially for performance-critical applications like games.
  • Operating systems provide convenience but introduce overhead and potential limitations.
  • Increasing abstraction in operating systems can lead to performance bottlenecks.
  • Simpler operating systems or custom environments can offer greater control and performance.
  • Balancing portability with performance is a key challenge.
  • Modern hardware requires operating systems to effectively manage multi-core processors and GPUs.
  • Developers should choose operating systems based on specific application requirements.
  • Performance and control are valued, suggesting specialized systems can be more effective.

Is Planet Nine Alone in the Outer System?

This article from Centauri Dreams explores the ongoing search for Planet Nine, a hypothetical planet believed to exist in the outer solar system. The article discusses a new study by Man Ho Chan, a researcher at the Education University of Hong Kong, which investigates the possibility of additional, smaller "Planet X" objects existing alongside Planet Nine.

The primary evidence for Planet Nine comes from the peculiar clustering of the orbits of several Trans-Neptunian Objects (TNOs). These TNOs, located beyond Neptune, exhibit orbital alignments that suggest the gravitational influence of a massive, unseen object. Planet Nine is theorized to be several times the mass of Earth, with a highly elongated orbit that takes it far beyond the known planets. However, despite extensive searches, Planet Nine has not yet been directly observed, leading some to question its existence or explore alternative explanations for the TNOs' orbital patterns.

Chan's study delves into the dynamics of the outer solar system, considering the potential for Planet Nine to shepherd other smaller objects into stable, co-orbital configurations. Using computer simulations, Chan explored a range of masses for Planet Nine, from 5 to 20 Earth masses, and investigated the long-term stability of hypothetical smaller planets sharing its orbital space. The simulations considered the gravitational influence of the known giant planets (Jupiter, Saturn, Uranus, and Neptune) and tracked the orbital evolution of the smaller objects over extended periods.

The study found that if Planet Nine exists, it could indeed support a population of smaller, co-orbital planets. These smaller planets would tend to cluster around stable Lagrangian points associated with Planet Nine's orbit. Lagrangian points are locations in space where the gravitational forces of two large bodies (in this case, the Sun and Planet Nine) create regions of stability. Objects located near these points tend to remain in those regions, co-orbiting with the larger body.

However, the simulations also revealed that the mass of Planet Nine plays a crucial role in determining the stability of these smaller co-orbital planets. If Planet Nine is too massive (around 20 Earth masses), the smaller objects tend to be ejected from the system due to strong gravitational perturbations. On the other hand, if Planet Nine is less massive (around 5 Earth masses), the smaller objects can maintain stable co-orbital relationships for extended periods.

The study suggests that the existence of smaller, co-orbital planets could provide further clues in the search for Planet Nine. If astronomers could detect these smaller objects, it would strengthen the case for the existence of Planet Nine and help to refine estimates of its mass and orbital parameters. The article concludes by emphasizing the need for continued observational efforts to explore the outer solar system and search for both Planet Nine and any potential co-orbital companions.

Many ransomware strains will abort if they detect a Russian keyboard installed (2021)

The article on KrebsOnSecurity discusses a simple yet effective method for preventing Russian hackers from compromising email accounts. This technique involves adding a Unicode character — specifically a non-printing character — to the beginning of the email account password. This seemingly minor alteration can disrupt the automated password-cracking tools frequently used by Russian hackers.

The author explains that Russian hacking groups often rely on lists of previously exposed usernames and passwords (obtained from data breaches) to attempt to access email accounts. They employ automated scripts and password-spraying techniques, trying common variations of passwords across numerous accounts. The insertion of a Unicode character throws off these automated systems because they are not programmed to handle such characters at the beginning of passwords. While a human manually entering the password would not notice the character, the automated script fails to authenticate, thus blocking the unauthorized access attempt.

The article emphasizes that this method is not a foolproof solution against all forms of hacking. It primarily defends against automated attacks that use predictable password lists and cracking techniques. More sophisticated attacks, such as targeted phishing campaigns or exploits of software vulnerabilities, would not be mitigated by this tactic. However, given the prevalence of automated password attacks, particularly from Russian hacking groups, this simple countermeasure can significantly increase the security of email accounts.

The author tested this method and confirmed its effectiveness in preventing automated login attempts using compromised credentials. He notes that while some websites might strip out Unicode characters, many popular email providers (like Gmail and Yahoo) allow them. He encourages readers to try adding a Unicode character to their passwords to enhance their protection against common hacking attempts.

The article also advises readers to use strong, unique passwords for each of their online accounts and enable multi-factor authentication (MFA) whenever possible. MFA provides an additional layer of security by requiring a second verification method, such as a code sent to a mobile device, making it significantly harder for hackers to gain access even if they have the correct password. The Unicode trick is presented as a supplementary defense, not a replacement for robust password practices and MFA.

Furthermore, the article implicitly highlights the ongoing threat posed by Russian state-sponsored hackers and their persistent efforts to compromise online accounts for espionage, political interference, and financial gain. By publicizing this simple countermeasure, the author aims to empower individuals to take proactive steps to defend themselves against these threats.

The article also briefly touches upon the importance of staying informed about data breaches and checking whether your email address has been compromised using services like "Have I Been Pwned?". If your credentials have been exposed in a breach, it's crucial to change your password immediately and implement additional security measures.

In conclusion, the article promotes a simple yet surprisingly effective technique to thwart automated password-cracking attacks from Russian hackers: prepending a Unicode character to email account passwords. While not a panacea, this method can significantly increase security when used in conjunction with strong passwords, multi-factor authentication, and vigilance regarding data breaches.

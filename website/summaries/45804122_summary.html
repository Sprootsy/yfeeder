The Mack Super Pumper was a locomotive engined fire fighter (2018)

Summary

The article discusses the Mack Super Pumper System, a massive firefighting apparatus developed by the New York City Fire Department (FDNY) in the 1960s. This system was designed to combat large-scale fires that standard firefighting equipment couldn't handle effectively. The core of the system consisted of the Super Pumper, a custom-built Mack truck carrying a monstrous pump powered by a 12-cylinder ALCO 251 diesel engine, typically found in locomotives. This engine delivered an astounding 880 horsepower, enabling the Super Pumper to pump up to 8,800 gallons of water per minute.

Complementing the Super Pumper was the Super Tender, also a custom Mack truck, carrying 4,500 feet of specialized 12-inch diameter hose. This hose, much larger than standard fire hoses, was essential for delivering the massive water flow from the Super Pumper to the fire scene. The Super Tender also carried specialized nozzles and other equipment.

The system's high pumping capacity allowed firefighters to deliver an unprecedented volume of water to fires, overwhelming the blaze and preventing it from spreading. The Super Pumper System was deployed at numerous major fires in New York City, proving its effectiveness in controlling and extinguishing even the most challenging infernos.

The article details the specifications of both the Super Pumper and the Super Tender, highlighting the unique design features and the ALCO engine's capabilities. It also recounts the system's operational history and its impact on firefighting tactics. While the Super Pumper System was eventually retired due to its high maintenance costs and the evolving nature of firefighting, it remains a legendary example of innovative engineering and a testament to the FDNY's commitment to protecting the city.

Show HN: I built a knife steel comparison tool

Summary

The article provides a comprehensive overview of various knife steels, comparing their properties and suitability for different knife applications. It delves into the key characteristics that define a steel's performance, such as hardness, toughness, wear resistance, and corrosion resistance. The author emphasizes that no single steel is perfect for all purposes and that the best choice depends on the intended use of the knife and the user's preferences.

The article begins by explaining the basic composition of steel, highlighting the role of carbon in increasing hardness and the impact of other alloying elements like chromium, vanadium, molybdenum, and nickel. It discusses how these elements influence properties like corrosion resistance, grain structure, and heat treatability. The author also touches upon the importance of the heat treatment process in achieving the desired properties from a particular steel.

The article then explores a wide range of specific knife steels, categorizing them roughly by type and performance level. These include common stainless steels like 420HC, AUS-8, and 8Cr13MoV, which are known for their good corrosion resistance and ease of sharpening, but may lack in edge retention. It also discusses more premium stainless steels such as VG-10, 154CM, CTS-XHP, and S30V, highlighting their improved edge retention and wear resistance. The article covers high-end stainless steels such as S35VN, S45VN, M390, and CPM-20CV, known for their excellent balance of properties, including high hardness, toughness, and corrosion resistance.

In addition to stainless steels, the article examines carbon steels like 1095 and tool steels like D2, CPM-M4, and CPM-CruWear. These steels generally offer higher hardness and toughness than many stainless steels, but they are more susceptible to corrosion and require more careful maintenance. The author discusses the trade-offs between these different types of steel, noting that carbon steels and tool steels are often preferred for applications where edge retention and toughness are paramount, while stainless steels are favored for their resistance to rust and ease of care.

Throughout the article, the author emphasizes the importance of understanding the specific properties of each steel and how they relate to the intended use of the knife. He also points out that factors such as the knife's geometry, heat treatment, and the user's sharpening skills can significantly impact its overall performance, regardless of the steel used.

The article aims to provide a broad overview of the knife steel landscape, enabling readers to make more informed decisions when choosing a knife. It acknowledges the complexity of the subject and encourages readers to continue learning and experimenting to find the steels that best suit their individual needs and preferences.

Key Points

  • Knife steel selection depends on balancing hardness, toughness, wear resistance, and corrosion resistance. No single steel excels in every category.
  • Carbon is the primary element for increasing hardness in steel. Other alloying elements (chromium, vanadium, molybdenum, nickel) affect corrosion resistance, grain structure, and heat treatability.
  • Heat treatment is crucial for achieving the desired properties of a specific steel.
  • Common stainless steels (420HC, AUS-8, 8Cr13MoV) offer good corrosion resistance and ease of sharpening, but may lack edge retention.
  • Premium stainless steels (VG-10, 154CM, CTS-XHP, S30V) improve edge retention and wear resistance.
  • High-end stainless steels (S35VN, S45VN, M390, CPM-20CV) offer an excellent balance of hardness, toughness, and corrosion resistance.
  • Carbon steels (1095) and tool steels (D2, CPM-M4, CPM-CruWear) generally provide higher hardness and toughness but are more prone to corrosion.
  • Knife geometry, heat treatment quality, and sharpening skill significantly affect knife performance, regardless of the steel.

Alzheimer's disrupts circadian rhythms of plaque-clearing brain cells

Summary:

A recent study from Washington University School of Medicine in St. Louis has revealed that Alzheimer's disease disrupts the daily rhythms of microglia, the brain's immune cells responsible for clearing amyloid plaques. This disruption impairs their ability to clear these plaques effectively. The research, conducted on mice, showed that healthy microglia exhibit a strong circadian rhythm, ramping up their activity during the day to clear amyloid beta and then resting at night. However, in mice with Alzheimer's-like pathology, this rhythm was significantly dampened, resulting in reduced plaque clearance. The study highlights that the circadian clock, which regulates various biological processes, is crucial for the proper functioning of microglia. When this clock is disrupted, microglia become less efficient at their primary task of removing amyloid plaques, a hallmark of Alzheimer's disease. The researchers observed that in healthy mice, the expression of genes involved in amyloid beta clearance fluctuated significantly throughout the day, peaking during waking hours. In contrast, in mice with amyloid plaques, these fluctuations were blunted, indicating a loss of circadian regulation. Furthermore, the researchers found that restoring the circadian rhythm in microglia could potentially improve their ability to clear amyloid plaques. They are now exploring ways to boost the circadian clock in microglia as a therapeutic strategy for Alzheimer's disease. This could involve developing drugs that target specific genes involved in the circadian rhythm or using other methods to reset the microglia's internal clock. The findings suggest that maintaining or restoring healthy circadian rhythms could be a novel approach to preventing or slowing the progression of Alzheimer's disease by enhancing the brain's natural ability to clear amyloid plaques. The study underscores the importance of the circadian clock in immune function within the brain and opens new avenues for therapeutic intervention in Alzheimer's disease.

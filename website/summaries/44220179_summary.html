Riding high in Germany on the world's oldest suspended railway

The article describes a visit to Wuppertal, Germany, to ride the Schwebebahn, the world's oldest suspended railway. The author recounts their initial skepticism about Wuppertal as a travel destination, having only known it for its IKEA store. However, they were drawn to the city by the unique Schwebebahn, which has been transporting passengers since 1901.

The author details the history of the railway, explaining how it was conceived to address the challenges of building a traditional railway in Wuppertal's densely populated and industrialized valley. The suspended design, proposed by Eugen Langen, was chosen for its minimal footprint and ability to navigate the river and built-up areas.

The article highlights the experience of riding the Schwebebahn, describing the sensation of floating above the city, passing over the Wupper River and through industrial and residential areas. The author notes the mix of architectural styles, from Wilhelminian-era buildings to post-war structures, and the glimpses into daily life as seen from the train.

The piece also touches on some notable incidents in the railway's history, including the famous 1950 incident where a young circus elephant named Tuffi jumped from the train into the river. The author also mentions a more recent incident in 1999 when a metal claw fell from a construction site and pierced a train car.

The author reflects on the Schwebebahn's enduring appeal and its integration into the daily lives of Wuppertal residents. They conclude that the railway is not just a mode of transport but also a symbol of the city's industrial heritage and a unique travel experience. The article encourages readers to consider Wuppertal as a destination and to ride the Schwebebahn for themselves.

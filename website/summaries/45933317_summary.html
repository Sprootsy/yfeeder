Summary

This NBER working paper, "The Price Effects of Input Trade Liberalization: Evidence from India," investigates the impact of India's input trade liberalization policies in the 1990s on manufacturing plant-level prices. The study leverages detailed plant-level data from the Annual Survey of Industries (ASI) combined with customs data to examine how tariff reductions on intermediate inputs affected the output prices charged by manufacturing firms. The core argument is that lower input costs, resulting from reduced tariffs, should translate into lower output prices, benefiting consumers and potentially enhancing the competitiveness of Indian manufacturing.

The authors construct a comprehensive dataset that links input tariffs to specific industries, allowing them to estimate the effect of these tariff changes on plant-level output prices. They employ various econometric techniques, including ordinary least squares (OLS) and instrumental variables (IV) regressions, to address potential endogeneity issues. The primary identification strategy involves using lagged tariffs and exploiting the variation in tariff reductions across different industries and over time. The instrumental variable approach uses the initial pre-reform tariff levels as instruments for subsequent tariff changes, addressing concerns that tariff reductions might be correlated with unobserved industry-specific shocks.

The study's findings reveal a significant negative relationship between input tariff reductions and output prices. Specifically, the authors find that a 10 percentage point reduction in input tariffs leads to a reduction in output prices of approximately 1-2%. This effect is economically meaningful and suggests that input trade liberalization played a substantial role in lowering the prices of manufactured goods in India. The results are robust across various specifications, including controls for industry-specific trends, plant-level characteristics, and potential confounding factors.

Furthermore, the paper explores the heterogeneity of these effects across different types of firms. It finds that the price-reducing effect of input tariff liberalization is more pronounced for firms that are more import-intensive and those that operate in more competitive industries. This suggests that firms that rely heavily on imported inputs and face greater competitive pressures are more likely to pass on the cost savings from lower input tariffs to consumers in the form of lower prices. Additionally, the study examines the impact on different types of goods, finding that the price effects are stronger for differentiated goods compared to homogeneous goods.

The authors also investigate the potential mechanisms through which input tariff reductions affect output prices. They find evidence that lower input costs lead to increased productivity and efficiency gains within firms, which in turn contribute to lower output prices. This suggests that input trade liberalization not only reduces direct input costs but also fosters improvements in firm-level productivity, leading to further price reductions.

In summary, the paper provides compelling evidence that input trade liberalization in India during the 1990s had a significant and beneficial impact on manufacturing plant-level prices. The findings highlight the importance of trade liberalization policies in promoting competition, reducing costs, and ultimately benefiting consumers. The study contributes to the broader literature on the effects of trade on firm behavior and provides valuable insights for policymakers considering trade reforms in developing countries.

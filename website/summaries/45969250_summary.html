Pebble, Rebble, and a path forward

Summary:

The article discusses the history of Pebble, its acquisition by Fitbit, and the subsequent rise of Rebble as a community-driven effort to keep Pebble watches functioning after Fitbit shut down Pebble's services. It delves into the technical challenges and achievements of the Rebble team in reverse engineering Pebble's cloud services and creating viable alternatives. The author, Eric Migicovsky, co-founder of Pebble, reflects on the lessons learned from Pebble's journey and proposes a vision for the future of independent, open-source smartwatch platforms. The article highlights the importance of community, the value of user control and data ownership, and the potential for creating a sustainable ecosystem around open smartwatch platforms.

The article starts by recounting the early days of Pebble, emphasizing its focus on simplicity, long battery life, and open development. It describes how Pebble gained a passionate following and disrupted the smartwatch market. However, despite its initial success, Pebble faced challenges from larger, well-funded competitors like Apple and Google. Ultimately, Pebble was acquired by Fitbit in 2016, and Fitbit subsequently shut down Pebble's cloud services, rendering many of Pebble's features useless.

In response to this, a group of dedicated Pebble users and developers formed Rebble. The Rebble team embarked on the ambitious task of reverse engineering Pebble's cloud services and building replacements. This involved recreating functionalities like app store access, voice recognition, and timeline features. The article praises the Rebble team's technical expertise and dedication, noting that they successfully kept Pebble watches alive and functional for many users.

The author then transitions to reflecting on the broader implications of Pebble's story. He emphasizes the importance of user control and data ownership. He argues that users should have the ability to choose the services they use with their devices and that they should own their own data. He critiques the walled-garden approach of large tech companies, where users are locked into a single ecosystem and their data is controlled by the company.

Looking to the future, the author proposes a vision for a new generation of independent, open-source smartwatch platforms. He suggests that these platforms should be built around open standards, allowing for interoperability and user choice. He also emphasizes the importance of community involvement and collaboration. He believes that a sustainable ecosystem can be created around open smartwatch platforms, where users, developers, and manufacturers can all benefit.

The article concludes by calling for a renewed focus on user empowerment and open innovation in the smartwatch space. It suggests that the lessons learned from Pebble and Rebble can serve as a guide for building a more decentralized, user-centric future for wearable technology.

  • Pebble was a successful early smartwatch that prioritized simplicity, battery life, and open development.

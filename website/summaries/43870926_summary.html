Why our waistlines expand in middle age: aging stem cells shift into overdrive

The article discusses a study published in Cell Stem Cell that explores the link between increased waistlines in middle age and the aging process. Researchers at the University of California, San Diego, led by Dr. Alexis Labb√©, investigated why abdominal fat tends to accumulate as people age, even without significant changes in diet or exercise. The study focused on mesenchymal stem cells (MSCs) found in fat tissue and their role in fat storage.

The research team discovered that as people age, MSCs become less efficient at converting energy into heat, a process called thermogenesis. This decline in thermogenic capacity leads to the storage of excess energy as fat, contributing to increased abdominal fat. The study involved analyzing MSCs from younger and older mice, revealing that older MSCs had a reduced ability to express genes involved in thermogenesis. Further experiments showed that transplanting older MSCs into younger mice resulted in fat accumulation in the recipients, confirming the causal role of MSC aging in increased fat storage.

The scientists also identified a potential mechanism behind this age-related decline in MSC function. They found that the levels of a protein called sLR11 were elevated in older MSCs. sLR11 inhibits thermogenesis by interfering with the activity of a key signaling pathway involving TGF-beta. Blocking sLR11 in older MSCs restored their thermogenic capacity, suggesting that sLR11 could be a therapeutic target for preventing or reversing age-related abdominal fat accumulation.

The study provides valuable insights into the cellular and molecular mechanisms driving age-related changes in body composition. By identifying sLR11 as a key regulator of MSC function, the research opens up new avenues for developing interventions to combat abdominal obesity and promote healthy aging. The findings suggest that targeting sLR11 or related pathways could potentially help maintain a healthy metabolism and prevent the adverse health consequences associated with increased abdominal fat in middle age.

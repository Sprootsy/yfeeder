Study finds gaps in evidence for air-cleaning technologies to prevent infections

Summary

A recent study from the University of Colorado Anschutz Medical Campus has identified significant gaps in the scientific evidence supporting the effectiveness of various air cleaning technologies aimed at preventing respiratory infections, including COVID-19. The research team, led by Dr. Marina Vance, conducted a systematic review of 118 studies that evaluated technologies like HEPA filters, ultraviolet germicidal irradiation (UVGI), bipolar ionization, and others.

The study found that while many technologies show promise in laboratory settings, there is a lack of high-quality evidence demonstrating their real-world impact on reducing respiratory infections in buildings. Many studies suffered from methodological limitations such as the absence of control groups, lack of blinding, and failure to account for other factors that influence infection rates, like ventilation and human behavior.

Specifically, the review highlighted that HEPA filters have a solid track record, primarily due to their mechanical removal of particles, but more real-world studies are needed. UVGI also showed potential, but questions remain about its effectiveness in occupied spaces and the potential for UV exposure. Technologies like bipolar ionization and plasma-based approaches had limited and often conflicting evidence, raising concerns about potential byproducts and the need for more rigorous testing.

The researchers emphasize the importance of using a multi-layered approach to reduce the spread of respiratory viruses, including vaccination, masking, and ventilation, in addition to air cleaning technologies. They call for more robust and standardized research to assess the real-world effectiveness of these technologies, including randomized controlled trials conducted in diverse building settings. They also encourage caution in marketing and deploying air cleaning technologies without sufficient evidence of their efficacy. The study's findings are particularly relevant for building owners, facility managers, and public health officials who are making decisions about investments in air cleaning technologies to protect building occupants.

Key Points

  • Gaps in Evidence: The study revealed significant gaps in the evidence supporting the effectiveness of air cleaning technologies in preventing respiratory infections in real-world settings.
  • Methodological Limitations: Many studies lacked methodological rigor, including the absence of control groups and blinding, making it difficult to draw definitive conclusions.
  • HEPA Filters: HEPA filters have a solid track record, but more real-world studies are needed to assess their effectiveness in diverse settings.
  • UVGI: UVGI shows potential, but concerns remain about its effectiveness in occupied spaces and potential for UV exposure.
  • Bipolar Ionization and Plasma: Technologies like bipolar ionization and plasma-based approaches had limited and often conflicting evidence.
  • Multi-Layered Approach: A multi-layered approach, including vaccination, masking, and ventilation, is essential to reduce the spread of respiratory viruses.
  • Call for More Research: The researchers call for more robust and standardized research, including randomized controlled trials, to assess the real-world effectiveness of air cleaning technologies.
  • Caution in Marketing: Caution is advised in marketing and deploying air cleaning technologies without sufficient evidence of their efficacy.

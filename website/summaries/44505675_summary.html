US court strikes down 'click-to-cancel' rule designed to make unsubscribing easy

Summary:

The article reports on a recent ruling by a federal appeals court that upholds a California law designed to protect consumers from automatically renewing subscriptions and memberships. The law, known as the “automatic renewal law,” mandates that businesses offering auto-renewal services online must provide consumers with clear and conspicuous terms, including how to cancel the service. The ruling specifically addressed a challenge to the law brought by online businesses who argued that it violated the Commerce Clause of the U.S. Constitution by imposing undue burdens on interstate commerce. The court rejected this argument, finding that the law only regulates businesses operating within California or targeting California consumers and that the burden on interstate commerce was not excessive in relation to the legitimate local benefits of protecting consumers from unwanted automatic renewals.

The case stemmed from concerns that many online subscriptions and memberships enroll customers in automatic renewals without adequately informing them of the terms or providing easy cancellation options. This can lead to consumers being charged repeatedly for services they no longer want or need. California's law aims to address this issue by requiring businesses to: (1) clearly and conspicuously disclose the automatic renewal terms before a consumer agrees to them; (2) obtain the consumer's affirmative consent to the terms; and (3) provide an easy-to-use mechanism for cancellation, such as a simple online link or button. The law also requires businesses to send consumers a reminder notice before the renewal occurs, giving them another opportunity to cancel.

The legal challenge to the law centered on the argument that it imposed significant compliance costs on online businesses, particularly those operating nationwide. Businesses argued that they would have to tailor their online subscription and cancellation processes to comply with California's specific requirements, which would be burdensome and expensive. They also argued that the law could lead to inconsistent regulations across different states, creating a patchwork of legal requirements that would make it difficult for businesses to operate online. The court, however, found that the benefits of the law in protecting California consumers outweighed these concerns. The court noted that other states have similar laws and that the burden on businesses was not so great as to render the law unconstitutional.

The ruling is seen as a significant victory for consumer protection advocates, who have long argued that automatic renewals can be deceptive and harmful. They contend that the California law provides a necessary safeguard against unfair business practices and that it empowers consumers to make informed decisions about their subscriptions and memberships. The ruling reinforces the ability of states to enact consumer protection laws that regulate online business practices, even if those businesses operate across state lines. It also sends a message to online businesses that they must be transparent and provide easy cancellation options for automatic renewals.

The decision may have implications for similar consumer protection laws in other states, as it affirms the constitutionality of state laws regulating online business practices. It could also encourage other states to enact similar laws to protect their consumers from unwanted automatic renewals. Ultimately, the ruling underscores the importance of clear and conspicuous disclosure in online transactions and the need for businesses to provide easy and accessible cancellation options for recurring subscriptions and memberships.

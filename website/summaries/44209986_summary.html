The startling rise of disability in America (2013)

This article is an investigative report focusing on how employers use personality tests to screen job applicants. These tests, often touted as scientifically valid, are used to narrow down applicant pools, sometimes disqualifying candidates based on personality traits deemed undesirable for a specific role or company culture. The investigation reveals that the criteria used in these tests can be arbitrary and discriminatory, potentially excluding qualified individuals and reinforcing biases in hiring practices. The tests often measure traits that are not necessarily related to job performance and can be easily manipulated by test-takers who try to guess the "right" answers.

The article highlights the lack of regulation and oversight of these tests, which are used across various industries and job levels. It emphasizes the potential for these tests to disproportionately impact individuals from marginalized groups. Because the tests may value conformity or specific communication styles, applicants from diverse backgrounds or those with neurodivergent traits may be unfairly disadvantaged.

Furthermore, the report delves into the history of personality testing and its evolution in the workplace. It traces the origins of these tests back to early 20th-century efforts to classify and sort individuals, and examines how they have been adapted and marketed for use in hiring decisions. The article also raises concerns about the validity and reliability of these tests, questioning whether they accurately predict job success and whether they are truly objective measures of personality.

The investigation includes interviews with job applicants who have been rejected based on personality test results, as well as experts in the field of industrial-organizational psychology. These interviews provide firsthand accounts of the impact of personality testing on individuals' career prospects and shed light on the ethical considerations surrounding their use. The report also examines the legal implications of using personality tests in hiring, noting that while some regulations exist, they may not adequately address the potential for discrimination and bias.

Overall, the article raises significant questions about the fairness, accuracy, and ethical implications of using personality tests as a screening tool in the hiring process. It calls for greater scrutiny of these tests and a more critical examination of their role in shaping employment opportunities.

Key Points:

  • Employers widely use personality tests to screen job applicants.
  • These tests may be arbitrary, discriminatory, and lack scientific validity.

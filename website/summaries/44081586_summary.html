Good Writing

Paul Graham's essay "Good Writing" argues that the key to good writing is simplicity, clarity, and honesty. He advises writers to focus on conveying their thoughts as clearly and directly as possible, rather than trying to impress readers with complex vocabulary or convoluted sentence structures. Good writing, according to Graham, is like a windowpane, allowing the reader to see the idea without being distracted by the writing itself. He criticizes writing that prioritizes sounding smart over being understood, often found in academic or bureaucratic contexts.

The essay emphasizes the importance of revision and rewriting. Graham suggests that the first draft is primarily for getting the ideas down, and the real work of writing happens in subsequent revisions, where the writer refines the language, eliminates unnecessary jargon, and ensures that the argument flows logically. He also highlights the value of showing your writing to others and incorporating their feedback.

Graham believes that good writing stems from having something interesting to say and wanting to share it with others. He advocates for writing about topics that genuinely interest the writer, as this enthusiasm will naturally translate into more engaging and effective prose. He warns against writing solely to impress others, as this often leads to stilted and artificial writing.

The essay encourages writers to use language that is natural and conversational. Graham suggests imagining that you are explaining the idea to a friend and writing in that same tone. He discourages the use of overly formal language or complex sentence structures, as these can make the writing difficult to understand and can distance the reader from the writer.

Graham also discusses the importance of honesty in writing. He believes that good writing should be authentic and reflect the writer's true thoughts and feelings. He warns against trying to adopt a persona or writing in a way that is inconsistent with your own voice. He also stresses that the reader can usually tell when you are not being authentic.

He also touches on the topic of grammar and style. While he acknowledges the importance of correct grammar, he argues that it should not be the primary focus of the writer. He believes that clarity and simplicity are more important than strict adherence to grammatical rules. He advises writers to focus on getting their ideas across clearly and to worry about grammar later.

Finally, Graham advises writers to read widely and to pay attention to the writing styles of authors they admire. He believes that reading good writing is one of the best ways to improve your own writing. He also suggests that writers should experiment with different styles and techniques to find what works best for them.

Key Points:

  • Simplicity and Clarity: Prioritize clear and direct communication over complex language.
  • Revision is Key: The real work of writing happens in rewriting and refining.
  • Write About What Interests You: Enthusiasm for the subject translates into better writing.
  • Use Natural Language: Write as you would speak to a friend.
  • Be Honest and Authentic: Let your true voice come through in your writing.
  • Don't Obsess Over Grammar Initially: Focus on clarity first, then refine grammar.
  • Read Widely: Learn from the writing styles of authors you admire.
  • Avoid Trying to Impress: Write to explain, not to show off.

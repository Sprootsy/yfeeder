The Xerox Alto, Smalltalk, and rewriting a running GUI (2017)

Summary

The article delves into the history of the Xerox Alto, Smalltalk, and the significant effort involved in rewriting the operating system and Smalltalk environment for this pioneering personal computer.

The Xerox Alto, developed in the early 1970s at Xerox PARC (Palo Alto Research Center), was a revolutionary machine that introduced many concepts now fundamental to personal computing, such as the graphical user interface (GUI), the mouse, and Ethernet networking. However, the Alto was not a commercial product and remained primarily within Xerox PARC and select universities.

Smalltalk was an object-oriented programming language and environment also developed at Xerox PARC. It was designed to be highly interactive and dynamic, with features like garbage collection and just-in-time compilation. Smalltalk was tightly integrated with the Alto's hardware and operating system.

The original Alto operating system and Smalltalk environment were written in a combination of languages, including BCPL (a precursor to C) for the operating system and a custom microcode for performance-critical parts of Smalltalk. As the system evolved and new features were added, the codebase became increasingly complex and difficult to maintain.

The article focuses on the ambitious project to rewrite the Alto operating system and Smalltalk environment. The primary motivation was to improve the system's reliability, maintainability, and performance. The rewrite involved reimplementing the operating system in a more structured language (likely a more modern variant of BCPL or an early version of C), and rewriting the Smalltalk virtual machine and libraries to take better advantage of the Alto's hardware.

The rewrite was a substantial undertaking, requiring a deep understanding of the existing system and careful planning to ensure compatibility and avoid introducing new bugs. The team faced challenges such as limited memory, slow processors, and the need to preserve the interactive and dynamic nature of Smalltalk. They had to rewrite large portions of the system while keeping it running and usable for the researchers at PARC.

The article highlights the technical challenges and trade-offs involved in such a rewrite, as well as the importance of a well-defined architecture and a skilled team.

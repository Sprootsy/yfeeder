Doge Put a College Student in Charge of Using AI to Rewrite Regulations

The article discusses how a college student named Alex Qin used AI to rewrite federal regulations, specifically those related to the Department of Energy's appliance efficiency standards. Frustrated by the dense and complex nature of these regulations, Qin sought to make them more accessible and understandable. He employed a combination of large language models (LLMs) like GPT-3.5 and GPT-4, along with custom Python scripts, to process, simplify, and reorganize the regulatory text.

Qin's project, initially a personal endeavor, gained attention after he posted about it on X (formerly Twitter). He aimed to "translate" the regulations into plain English, making them easier for the general public, policymakers, and even industry stakeholders to comprehend. The original regulations are often filled with jargon, cross-references, and legalistic language, making them difficult to navigate. Qin's AI-powered approach involved breaking down the regulations into smaller, more manageable chunks, summarizing key points, and creating a more logical flow of information.

The article highlights the potential of AI to democratize access to legal and regulatory information. By using LLMs to simplify complex text, Qin demonstrated how AI can bridge the gap between regulatory language and public understanding. The project also raises important questions about the role of AI in governance and the potential for AI-driven deregulation. While Qin's initial focus was on improving accessibility, the underlying technology could also be used to identify and eliminate redundant or outdated regulations.

However, the article also acknowledges the limitations and potential pitfalls of using AI for this purpose. LLMs are not perfect and can sometimes produce inaccurate or misleading summaries. Careful human oversight and validation are necessary to ensure the accuracy and reliability of the AI-generated rewrites. Additionally, there are concerns about the potential for misuse of AI in deregulation efforts, particularly if it is used to weaken or eliminate regulations that protect public health, safety, or the environment.

Qin's work serves as a proof-of-concept for how AI can be used to improve regulatory transparency and accessibility. It also sparks a broader discussion about the future of regulation in the age of AI, including the need for careful consideration of ethical and societal implications. The project underscores the importance of finding ways to make complex legal and regulatory information more accessible to the public, while also ensuring that AI is used responsibly and ethically in the regulatory process.

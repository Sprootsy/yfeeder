Should I Use a Carousel? (2013)

Summary

The article "Should I Use a Carousel?" critically examines the effectiveness and usability of carousels (also known as sliders) on websites. It argues that carousels are often a poor design choice, leading to negative user experiences and low engagement. The primary reason cited is that users frequently ignore or miss content within carousels due to banner blindness and the tendency to perceive them as advertisements. Furthermore, carousels can frustrate users because they often move too quickly, lack clear controls, or are difficult to navigate on mobile devices.

The article presents evidence from various sources, including user testing and analytics data, to support its claims. These findings consistently show that only a small percentage of users interact with carousels beyond the first slide. Even fewer users click on any of the carousel items. The author emphasizes that carousels can negatively impact website performance by increasing page load times, particularly if they contain large images or videos. This slow loading speed can lead to user frustration and higher bounce rates.

The author also discusses accessibility issues associated with carousels. Users with disabilities, such as those using screen readers or those with motor impairments, may find carousels difficult or impossible to use. In particular, automatically rotating carousels can be problematic for users who need more time to read and understand the content.

Instead of using carousels, the article suggests alternative design patterns that are more effective at presenting information. These include using static content blocks, well-designed landing pages, or clear calls to action to guide users to the most important information. The author recommends prioritizing content and presenting it in a way that is easily scannable and accessible to all users.

The article acknowledges that there might be specific cases where carousels could be considered such as displaying logos or testimonials. However, it stresses that even in these situations, careful consideration should be given to the user experience and accessibility. It recommends that if a carousel is used, it should be implemented with clear controls, appropriate timing, and full accessibility features.

In conclusion, the article strongly advises against using carousels in most situations, citing their negative impact on user engagement, performance, and accessibility. It advocates for more effective and user-friendly ways to present content on websites.

Key Points

  • Carousels often lead to banner blindness, causing users to ignore the content.
  • Analytics show low engagement rates with carousels beyond the first slide.
  • Carousels can negatively impact website performance by increasing page load times.
  • Carousels pose accessibility challenges for users with disabilities.
  • Alternative design patterns like static content blocks and well-designed landing pages are more effective.
  • If a carousel is used, it should have clear controls, appropriate timing, and full accessibility features.
  • The article generally advises against using carousels due to their negative impact on user experience.

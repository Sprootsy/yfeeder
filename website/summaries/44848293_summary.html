A CT scanner reveals surprises inside the 386 processor's ceramic package

Summary

The article is a detailed exploration of the internal structure of an Intel 386 DX processor, as revealed through a CT scan. The author obtained a 386 DX processor and subjected it to a CT scan to visualize its internal components and understand its construction.

The article begins by explaining the basics of CT scanning, where X-ray images are taken from multiple angles and combined to create a 3D representation of an object. The author then describes the process of scanning the 386 processor, highlighting the challenges and techniques used to obtain high-quality images.

The CT scan reveals the intricate layers of the processor, including the silicon die, the metal layers used for interconnects, and the ceramic package that houses the die. The author explains how these layers are constructed and how they contribute to the functionality of the processor. The silicon die is the most critical part, containing the transistors and logic gates that perform the processor's calculations. The metal layers provide the pathways for electrical signals to travel between different parts of the die, while the ceramic package provides mechanical support and thermal dissipation.

The author also delves into the different regions of the silicon die, identifying the key functional units such as the arithmetic logic unit (ALU), the control unit, and the memory cache. The CT scan allows the author to trace the paths of the metal interconnects, revealing how these units are connected and how data flows through the processor.

The article emphasizes the complexity of the 386 processor, noting that it contains hundreds of thousands of transistors arranged in a highly intricate pattern. The CT scan provides a unique perspective on this complexity, allowing the author to visualize the internal structure of the processor in a way that would not be possible with traditional methods.

In conclusion, the article offers a fascinating glimpse into the inner workings of an Intel 386 DX processor, showcasing the power of CT scanning as a tool for reverse engineering and understanding complex electronic devices.

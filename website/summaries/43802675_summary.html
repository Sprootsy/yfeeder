Backblaze: Mounting Losses, Lawsuits, Sham Accounting, Insider Selling

Summary

The article is a comprehensive analysis of Backblaze, a cloud storage and online backup provider, conducted by Morpheus Research. It delves into the company's history, business model, services, technology, competitive landscape, financial performance, and future prospects. The report aims to provide potential investors and industry observers with a detailed understanding of Backblaze's position in the market and its potential for growth.

Backblaze initially started as a solution to the founders' personal need for reliable and affordable data backup. This led to the creation of a service focused on simplicity and ease of use. Their core offerings include Backblaze Backup, which provides unlimited online backup for computers, and Backblaze B2 Cloud Storage, a more versatile cloud storage service comparable to Amazon S3 or Google Cloud Storage. A key differentiator for Backblaze has always been its pricing strategy, offering competitive rates that undercut larger players. They also offer services like Backblaze B2 Native API which gives developers the ability to manage their data directly via the command line interface. Additional offerings include server backup, the ability to ship hard drives containing backed-up data and partner API's.

The company's technology relies heavily on building its own storage hardware, which they refer to as "Storage Pods." These pods are designed for cost-effectiveness and high density, allowing Backblaze to optimize storage costs. They openly share the designs and specifications of these pods, contributing to the open-source hardware community and building brand trust. The Backblaze architecture consists of Vaults, which contain Storage Pods, and are designed for scale and redundancy. Backblaze also operates its own CDN to accelerate access to B2 data.

The competitive landscape for Backblaze includes large cloud providers like Amazon Web Services (AWS), Google Cloud Platform (GCP), and Microsoft Azure, as well as other backup and storage specialists. Backblaze competes primarily on price and ease of use, targeting individuals and small businesses who may find the larger platforms too complex or expensive. It must address the competition from companies like Carbonite, iDrive, and others to stay relevant.

The report suggests Backblaze has shown steady growth and solid financial performance, demonstrating the viability of its business model. In November of 2021 Backblaze became a public company with shares traded on NASDAQ. They have a long track record of operational transparency via their quarterly reports. The analysis also discusses Backblaze's potential for future expansion, including opportunities in new markets, services, and partnerships. Key challenges include maintaining its competitive edge in the face of increasing competition and scaling its infrastructure to meet growing demand.

Key Points

  • Core Business: Cloud storage and online backup services, primarily Backblaze Backup and Backblaze B2 Cloud Storage.
  • Value Proposition: Simplicity, ease of use, and competitive pricing.
  • Technology: Custom-built "Storage Pods" for cost-effective and high-density storage. Vault architecture is used to achieve scale and redundancy. Backblaze operates its own CDN to accelerate data access.
  • Competitive Landscape: Competes with major cloud providers (AWS, GCP, Azure) and specialized backup/storage companies.
  • Target Market: Individuals and small businesses seeking affordable and easy-to-use backup and storage solutions.
  • Financial Performance: Steady growth and solid financial performance, now a public company.
  • Future Opportunities: Expansion into new markets, services, and partnerships.
  • Challenges: Maintaining competitive edge, scaling infrastructure, and managing increasing competition.
  • Business Model: Focuses on a freemium/subscription model.
  • Data Center Locations: Sacramento, CA and Amsterdam, Netherlands.

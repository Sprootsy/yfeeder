A media company demanded a license fee for an Open Graph image I used

Summary

The article "Open Graph Licensing: A practical path to defining terms of use for your content" by Alistair Shepherd addresses the complexities of licensing content for use in Open Graph (OG) implementations, particularly when the destination website may not have a direct relationship with the content creator. It highlights the gap in clearly defined terms of use for content snippets, images, and other media shared via Open Graph tags and how this can lead to legal ambiguities.

The author begins by explaining the common scenario: a user shares a link to a piece of content (e.g., a news article, blog post, or product page) on a social media platform. The platform then uses Open Graph tags embedded in the linked page's HTML to generate a rich preview, typically including a title, description, and image. The issue arises because the content creator (the owner of the linked page) doesn't always explicitly grant the social media platform (or any other site displaying the OG data) the right to use their content in this way.

Shepherd argues that while fair use or similar legal doctrines might often cover such usage, relying solely on these defenses is not ideal due to their inherent ambiguity and potential for legal challenges. He suggests that content creators should proactively define the terms of use for their content when it's consumed via Open Graph.

The core of the article proposes a practical method for implementing clear licensing terms using a combination of HTML meta tags and a dedicated licensing page. The proposed solution involves adding specific Open Graph meta tags to the HTML of the content page. These tags point to a separate page on the content creator's website that explicitly outlines the terms of use for the content when it's accessed through Open Graph.

The suggested Open Graph tags include:

  • og:licensing: Points to the URL of the licensing terms page.
  • og:attribution: Allows specifying how attribution should be given when the content is used.
  • og:copywrite: specifies the copyright holder and year.

The licensing terms page itself should clearly state what rights are granted (e.g., the right to display the title, description, and image in a preview) and any restrictions (e.g., requirements for attribution, limitations on commercial use). The author provides example text for such a licensing page, covering aspects like permitted uses, attribution requirements, and disclaimers.

By implementing this approach, content creators can provide clarity and legal certainty regarding the use of their content in Open Graph implementations, reducing the risk of misunderstandings or legal disputes. The article concludes by emphasizing the importance of proactively addressing licensing concerns in the context of Open Graph and offers this method as a practical step towards achieving that goal.

Key Points

  • Open Graph implementations often lack clear terms of use for the content they display, leading to potential legal ambiguities.
  • Relying solely on fair use or similar legal doctrines is not ideal due to their inherent ambiguity.
  • Content creators should proactively define the terms of use for their content when it's consumed via Open Graph.
  • The proposed solution involves using Open Graph meta tags (og:licensing, og:attribution, og:copywrite) to point to a dedicated licensing page.
  • The licensing page should clearly state the permitted uses, attribution requirements, and any other relevant terms.
  • This approach provides clarity and legal certainty regarding the use of content in Open Graph implementations.

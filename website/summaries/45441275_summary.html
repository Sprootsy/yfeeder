ICE to Buy Tool That Tracks Locations of Hundreds Millions of Phones Every Day

Summary

According to a report by 404 Media, Immigration and Customs Enforcement (ICE) plans to purchase access to a powerful location-tracking tool called "Locate X." This tool, developed by data broker Venntel, can track the movements of hundreds of millions of mobile phones daily. Locate X aggregates location data harvested from various mobile apps, creating a massive database of individuals' whereabouts.

The acquisition of Locate X raises significant privacy concerns. The tool allows ICE to bypass traditional warrant requirements for tracking individuals, potentially enabling the agency to monitor the movements of both documented and undocumented immigrants, as well as U.S. citizens. The data is collected passively from apps on people's phones, often without explicit user consent or awareness that their location data is being shared with third parties like Venntel.

The article highlights the broad scope of Locate X's tracking capabilities. It can provide historical location data, real-time tracking, and pattern-of-life analysis. This level of surveillance raises concerns about potential abuse and the chilling effect it could have on freedom of movement and association. Critics argue that allowing ICE to access such a powerful tool without proper oversight undermines constitutional rights and democratic principles. The potential for errors and biases in the data also poses risks, as inaccurate location information could lead to wrongful targeting or detention.

The report emphasizes the lack of transparency surrounding ICE's use of location data and the limited legal framework governing the acquisition and use of such information. This lack of accountability makes it difficult to assess the potential impact of Locate X on individual privacy and civil liberties.

Key Points

  • ICE plans to purchase access to "Locate X," a location-tracking tool developed by Venntel.
  • Locate X tracks the location of hundreds of millions of mobile phones daily using data from various mobile apps.
  • The acquisition raises privacy concerns as it allows ICE to track individuals without warrants.
  • The tool enables historical location tracking, real-time monitoring, and pattern-of-life analysis.
  • Critics worry about potential abuse, lack of oversight, and the impact on civil liberties.
  • The report highlights the limited legal framework governing the use of location data by ICE.

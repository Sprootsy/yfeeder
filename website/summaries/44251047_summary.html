Research suggests Big Bang may have taken place inside a black hole

The article discusses an alternative theory to the Big Bang, suggesting that our universe may have originated inside a black hole. This theory addresses several issues with the standard Big Bang model, such as the singularity problem (where density and temperature become infinite) and the need for an inflationary period to explain the universe's uniformity.

The researchers propose that when a massive star collapses to form a black hole in a higher-dimensional universe, it creates a "cosmological singularity" within the black hole's event horizon. This singularity acts like the Big Bang, initiating the expansion of a new, lower-dimensional universe inside the black hole. From our perspective, we would exist within the event horizon of this black hole, unaware of the higher-dimensional universe outside.

This model solves the singularity problem because the singularity is contained within the black hole, shielded from the external universe. It also eliminates the need for inflation, as the intense gravitational forces within the black hole naturally lead to rapid expansion and homogenization of the nascent universe. Additionally, the black hole's spin could explain the observed asymmetry between matter and antimatter in our universe.

The theory also suggests that the cosmic microwave background (CMB) radiation, the afterglow of the Big Bang, could contain evidence of the black hole origin. Specifically, patterns in the CMB might reveal information about the mass, spin, and charge of the parent black hole.

While this model is still theoretical, it provides a compelling alternative to the standard Big Bang and addresses some of its major shortcomings. Further research and observations of the CMB are needed to test the validity of this intriguing hypothesis.

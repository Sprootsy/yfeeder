PWM flicker: Invisible light that's harming our health?

The article discusses the potential harm of blue light emitted from artificial light sources, particularly LED lighting and electronic devices. It begins by highlighting how humans evolved under natural light, which is balanced with red and infrared light, unlike modern artificial light that is heavily skewed towards blue light. The author explains that blue light, while beneficial during the day for alertness and mood, can disrupt our circadian rhythm and negatively impact sleep when exposure occurs at night.

The article details the mechanism through which blue light affects our bodies, focusing on melanopsin, a photoreceptor in the eye that is highly sensitive to blue light. Melanopsin plays a crucial role in regulating our sleep-wake cycle. Exposure to blue light suppresses melatonin production, a hormone that promotes sleep. Consequently, nighttime exposure to blue light can lead to insomnia, poor sleep quality, and other health issues related to circadian rhythm disruption.

Furthermore, the article points out that chronic disruption of the circadian rhythm due to excessive blue light exposure has been linked to a higher risk of various health problems, including obesity, diabetes, cardiovascular disease, and even certain types of cancer. It emphasizes that modern lifestyles, with increased screen time and reliance on LED lighting, have significantly increased our exposure to blue light, potentially contributing to these health concerns.

The author then explores the differences between various types of artificial lighting. Incandescent lights are presented as having a spectrum closer to natural light, although they are energy inefficient. Halogen lights are similar to incandescent but more efficient. Fluorescent lights are criticized for their uneven spectrum and potential health risks associated with mercury content and flicker. LED lights, while energy-efficient and long-lasting, are identified as a major source of blue light, especially “white” LEDs, which often use a blue LED with a yellow phosphor coating.

The article provides practical recommendations for mitigating the harmful effects of blue light. These include limiting screen time, especially in the evening, using blue light filtering glasses or screen protectors, and choosing warmer-toned lighting options with lower color temperatures (around 2700K or lower) for homes and workplaces. The author recommends using red or amber colored lights at night, which have minimal impact on melatonin production. There is also emphasis on increasing exposure to natural sunlight during the day to help regulate the circadian rhythm.

In conclusion, the article advocates for a more mindful approach to lighting, emphasizing the importance of mimicking natural light patterns to support our health and well-being. It suggests that by making informed choices about our lighting and screen usage, we can minimize the negative impacts of blue light and promote healthier sleep and overall health.

Key Points:

  • Blue light from artificial sources, especially LEDs and screens, can disrupt the circadian rhythm.
  • Melanopsin, a photoreceptor, is highly sensitive to blue light and regulates the sleep-wake cycle.
  • Blue light suppresses melatonin production, leading to sleep problems.
  • Chronic circadian rhythm disruption is linked to various health issues like obesity, diabetes, and cancer.
  • Different light sources have varying levels of blue light emission; LEDs are a significant source.
  • Strategies to mitigate blue light exposure include limiting screen time, using blue light filters, and choosing warmer-toned lighting.
  • Exposure to natural sunlight during the day is important for regulating the circadian rhythm.

10 Years of Pomological Watercolors

Summary:

The article is a reflective blog post by Parker Higgins celebrating the 10th anniversary of a project involving pomological watercolors. The project began with Higgins acquiring a collection of century-old watercolors of apples and pears from the USDA Pomological Watercolor Collection. These paintings, created by various artists, were initially used for record-keeping and scientific purposes, documenting fruit varieties.

Higgins digitized the collection, releasing them into the public domain for anyone to use. The blog post recounts the various ways these images have been used over the past decade. These uses include artistic endeavors such as tattoos, album covers, and fabric patterns, as well as educational applications in books, articles, and even a cider label.

The author expresses joy and surprise at the diverse and creative applications of the images, emphasizing the unexpected ways public domain resources can be repurposed and enjoyed. The post also touches on the importance of institutions like the USDA in preserving and making these resources accessible.

The author also notes the personal significance of the project, reflecting on how it has shaped their understanding of copyright, open access, and the value of preserving cultural heritage. The post serves as a testament to the power of open access and the enduring appeal of historical scientific illustration.
